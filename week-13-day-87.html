Negotiating Price and Terms

Day 85-86 taught you to build relationships and establish trust. Day 87 teaches you how to negotiate price and terms — from salary negotiations to vendor contracts. B2 speakers ask "Can you give me a discount?" C1 speakers negotiate with value-based arguments, conditional language, and strategic trade-offs.

Today's Philosophy

Negotiation = Value Arguments + Conditional Language + Strategic Trade-offs

"Negotiating isn't about getting the lowest price. It's about creating value for both sides, using conditional language to explore possibilities, and finding strategic trade-offs that benefit everyone."

Every negotiation requires you to make five decisions simultaneously:

Opening Decision: How do I open the negotiation? (Direct? Strategic?)
Value Decision: How do I argue value? (Price-focused? Value-focused?)
Conditional Decision: How do I use conditional language? (Direct? Conditional?)
Objection Decision: How do I handle objections? (Defensive? Strategic?)
Closing Decision: How do I close the deal? (Pushy? Collaborative?)

The Negotiation Loop

The 4-step process for negotiating price and terms

1
OPEN (Open the negotiation strategically)
How do I open? "I appreciate the offer, but I was hoping for..." / "I'd be open to X if..." (Open strategically)
2
ARGUE VALUE (Argue value, not just price)
How do I argue value? "Given our volume..." / "Given our long-term commitment..." (Argue value)
3
CONDITION (Use conditional language)
How do I use conditional language? "If we could X, would you be willing to Y?" (Use conditional language)
4
CLOSE (Close the deal collaboratively)
How do I close? "Let's see if we can find a middle ground" / "Can we agree on..." (Close collaboratively)

1

Opening the Negotiation

"How do I open the negotiation strategically?"

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High The Opening Decision

3-Level Examples

Simple (B2) "Can you give me a discount?"
Natural (B2+) "I appreciate the offer, but I was hoping for a better price."
C1 Professional "I appreciate the offer, and I can see the value in your product. I was hoping we could discuss the pricing — given our volume and long-term commitment, would you be open to discussing a more competitive rate?"

Life Example

Negotiating rent. Decision: Strategic opening ("I appreciate the property, and I can see the value. I was hoping we could discuss the rent — given my long-term commitment, would you be open to discussing a more competitive rate?")

⚖️ Trade-offs
Gain (Strategic) Shows professionalism, creates win-win, maintains relationship.
Lose (Strategic) Takes more time, requires preparation.

Common Mistake

Opening with direct demand ("Can you give me a discount?"). Native C1 speakers open strategically with value arguments.

Reflection: Am I opening strategically or with direct demands?

2

Arguing Value

"How do I argue value, not just price?"

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High The Value Decision

3-Level Examples

Simple (B2) "We're a big customer."
Natural (B2+) "We're planning to order a lot, so we should get a better price."
C1 Professional "Given our projected volume of 500 units per quarter and our commitment to a 2-year contract, we believe there's room for a more competitive pricing structure that reflects our long-term partnership."

Life Example

Negotiating car price. Decision: Value argument ("Given that I'm paying cash and can close this week, I believe there's room for a more competitive price that reflects the quick sale.")

Business Example

Negotiating salary. Decision: Professional value argument ("Given my 8 years of experience in this field and the value I can bring to this role, I believe there's room for a more competitive compensation package.")

⚖️ Trade-offs
Gain (Value-based) Creates win-win, justifies request, maintains relationship.
Lose (Value-based) Requires preparation and data.

Common Mistake

Arguing only price without value. Native C1 speakers argue value with specific data and long-term commitment.

Reflection: Am I arguing value or just price?

3

Using Conditional Language

"How do I use conditional language effectively?"

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High The Conditional Decision

3-Level Examples

Simple (B2) "If you give me a discount, I'll buy it."
Natural (B2+) "If you can offer a better price, I'm ready to sign today."
C1 Professional "If we could commit to a 2-year contract with quarterly orders of 500 units, would you be willing to offer a 15% discount on the standard rate? That would make this a win-win for both sides."

Life Example

Negotiating apartment lease. Decision: Conditional language ("If I could sign a 2-year lease today, would you be willing to offer a 10% discount on the monthly rent?")

Business Example

Negotiating vendor contract. Decision: Professional conditional language ("If we could commit to a 2-year contract, would you be willing to offer a 15% discount and include free implementation support?")

⚖️ Trade-offs
Gain (Conditional) Creates win-win, shows flexibility, maintains relationship.
Lose (Conditional) Requires giving something in return.

Common Mistake

Using direct demands without conditions. Native C1 speakers use conditional language to create win-win outcomes.

Reflection: Am I using conditional language or direct demands?

5

Closing the Deal

"How do I close the deal collaboratively?"

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High The Closing Decision

3-Level Examples

Simple (B2) "Can we agree on this price?"
Natural (B2+) "Can we agree on this price and move forward?"
C1 Professional "Let's see if we can find a middle ground that works for both sides. If we could agree on [X], would you be willing to move forward with the contract today? That would be a win-win for both sides."

Life Example

Negotiating apartment. Decision: Collaborative closing ("Let's see if we can find a middle ground. If we could agree on $1,500/month, would you be willing to move forward with the lease today?")

Business Example

Negotiating contract. Decision: Professional closing ("Let's see if we can find a middle ground. If we could agree on a 15% discount with a 2-year commitment, would you be willing to move forward with the contract today?")

️ Trade-offs
Gain (Collaborative) Creates win-win, maintains relationship, closes deal.
Lose (Collaborative) Takes more time, requires compromise.

Common Mistake

Pushing too hard or being too passive. Native C1 speakers close collaboratively with win-win outcomes.

Reflection: Am I closing collaboratively or pushing too hard?

3 Common Mistakes

Traps that destroy negotiations

Mistake 1: Opening with Direct Demands

❌ "Can you give me a discount?"
✅ "I appreciate the offer, and I can see the value. Given our volume and long-term commitment, would you be open to discussing a more competitive rate?"
Why: Direct demands create resistance. Strategic openings with value arguments create win-win outcomes.

Mistake 2: Arguing Only Price

❌ "That's too expensive. Can you lower the price?"
✅ "Given our projected volume and long-term commitment, we believe there's room for a more competitive pricing structure that reflects our partnership."
Why: Arguing only price creates adversarial relationship. Arguing value creates win-win.

Mistake 3: Getting Defensive

❌ "That's ridiculous. Your competitor offers much better."
✅ "I can see where you're coming from. However, given the competitive quotes we've received, I was hoping we could find a middle ground."
Why: Getting defensive destroys relationship. Strategic objection handling maintains relationship and keeps negotiation open.

🏠 Case 1: Life - Negotiating Rent with Landlord

Context: You're negotiating rent for a new apartment. The landlord quoted $1,800/month, but you were hoping for $1,600/month. You have good credit and can sign a 2-year lease.

You: "Thank you for showing me the apartment. I appreciate the property, and I can see the value. The location is great, and the amenities are exactly what I'm looking for."

Landlord: "Great! The rent is $1,800 per month, and we require a 1-year lease."

You: "I appreciate the offer, and I can see the value in this property. However, given my excellent credit history and willingness to sign a 2-year lease, would you be open to discussing a more competitive rate? I was hoping we could find a middle ground around $1,600 per month."

Landlord: "Well, $1,600 is quite a bit lower than our asking price. We usually don't go below $1,700."

You: "I can see where you're coming from, and I appreciate you explaining the value. However, given my excellent credit, stable income, and willingness to sign a 2-year lease, I was hoping we could find a middle ground. Would you be willing to meet at $1,650 per month with a 2-year lease? That would be a win-win for both sides."

Landlord: "Well, with a 2-year lease and your credit history, I could do $1,650 per month."

You: "That sounds reasonable. If we could agree on $1,650 per month with a 2-year lease, would you be willing to move forward with the lease today? That would be a win-win for both sides."

Landlord: "Yes, I think we can make that work."

You: "Perfect. Let's move forward with the lease at $1,650 per month for 2 years. Thank you for working with me on this."

💼 Case 2: Business - Negotiating Vendor Contract

Context: You're negotiating a vendor contract for software. The vendor quoted $50,000/year, but you were hoping for $40,000/year. You can commit to a 2-year contract with quarterly orders.

You: "Thank you for the proposal. I appreciate the proposal, and I can see the value in your solution. The features are exactly what we need, and the support looks comprehensive."

Vendor: "Great! The annual license is $50,000, and we require annual payment."

You: "I appreciate the offer, and I can see the value in your solution. However, given our projected volume of 500 users and willingness to commit to a 2-year contract, would you be open to discussing a more competitive pricing structure? We were hoping to find a middle ground around $40,000 per year."

Vendor: "Well, $40,000 is quite a bit lower than our standard pricing. We usually don't go below $45,000."

You: "I can see where you're coming from, and I appreciate you explaining the value. However, given our projected volume, 2-year commitment, and potential for expansion, I was hoping we could find a middle ground. Would you be willing to meet at $42,000 per year with a 2-year contract and quarterly payments? That would be a win-win for both sides."

Vendor: "Well, with a 2-year commitment and quarterly payments, I could do $42,000 per year."

You: "That sounds reasonable. If we could agree on $42,000 per year with a 2-year contract and quarterly payments, would you be willing to move forward with the contract today? That would be a win-win for both sides."

Vendor: "Yes, I think we can make that work."

You: "Perfect. Let's move forward with the contract at $42,000 per year for 2 years with quarterly payments. Thank you for working with me on this."

Micro Writing: Negotiation Messages (with Model Answers)

️ 4 Writing Tasks with Model Answers

Task 1: Negotiating Rent Email

Context: You're negotiating rent for a new apartment. The landlord quoted $1,800/month, but you were hoping for $1,600/month. Write an email negotiating the rent.

Requirements:
  • Use 3 collocations from this lesson
  • Use 2 conditional structures (e.g., "If we could X, would you be willing to Y?")
  • Use 2 value arguments (e.g., "Given my excellent credit...")
  • Length: 100-150 words
Model Answer (C1)

"Dear [Landlord], Thank you for showing me the apartment. I appreciate the property, and I can see the value. The location is great, and the amenities are exactly what I'm looking for. I appreciate the offer of $1,800 per month. However, given my excellent credit history and willingness to sign a 2-year lease, would you be open to discussing a more competitive rate? I was hoping we could find a middle ground around $1,600 per month. I can see where you're coming from, and I appreciate you explaining the value. However, given my excellent credit, stable income, and willingness to sign a 2-year lease, I was hoping we could find a middle ground. Would you be willing to meet at $1,650 per month with a 2-year lease? That would be a win-win for both sides."

Language Breakdown
  • Value Arguments: "Given my excellent credit history...", "Given my excellent credit, stable income..."
  • Conditional Structures: "Would you be open to discussing...", "Would you be willing to meet..."
  • Collocations: "competitive rate", "middle ground", "win-win"
  • Cohesion markers: "I appreciate", "I can see where you're coming from"
Alternative Version (Casual)

"Hi [Landlord], Thanks for showing me the apartment. I really like it, and I can see the value. I appreciate the offer of $1,800 per month. However, given my good credit and willingness to sign a 2-year lease, would you be open to discussing a better rate? I was hoping we could find a middle ground around $1,600 per month. Would you be willing to meet at $1,650 per month with a 2-year lease? That would be a win-win for both sides."

Self-check Checklist
  • □ đủ word count (100-150 words)
  • □ dùng đủ 3 collocations (competitive rate, middle ground, win-win)
  • □ dùng đủ 2 conditional structures (Would you be open to..., Would you be willing to...)
  • □ dùng đủ 2 value arguments (Given my excellent credit...)
  • □ có opening → development → closing

Integrated Dialogues (Long Format - 15-20 turns)

💬 Integrated Dialogues (15-20 turns with evolution)

🏠 Dialogue 1: Negotiating Rent (Life)

Context: You're negotiating rent for a new apartment. The landlord quoted $1,800/month, but you were hoping for $1,600/month. Conversation evolves through opening → value argument → conditional language → closing.
You:
"Thank you for showing me the apartment. I appreciate the property, and I can see the value. The location is great, and the amenities are exactly what I'm looking for." Open
Landlord:
"Great! The rent is $1,800 per month, and we require a 1-year lease."
You:
"I appreciate the offer, and I can see the value. However, given my excellent credit history and willingness to sign a 2-year lease, would you be open to discussing a more competitive rate? I was hoping we could find a middle ground around $1,600 per month." Argue Value
Landlord:
"Well, $1,600 is quite a bit lower than our asking price. We usually don't go below $1,700."
You:
"I can see where you're coming from, and I appreciate you explaining the value. However, given my excellent credit, stable income, and willingness to sign a 2-year lease, I was hoping we could find a middle ground. Would you be willing to meet at $1,650 per month with a 2-year lease? That would be a win-win for both sides." Condition
Landlord:
"Well, with a 2-year lease and your credit history, I could do $1,650 per month."
You:
"That sounds reasonable. If we could agree on $1,650 per month with a 2-year lease, would you be willing to move forward with the lease today? That would be a win-win for both sides." Close
Landlord:
"Yes, I think we can make that work."
You:
"Perfect. Let's move forward with the lease at $1,650 per month for 2 years. Thank you for working with me on this." Close

Decision Point Analysis

  • Decision: Combined Open (appreciate value) → Argue Value (credit, commitment) → Condition (2-year lease for better rate) → Close (win-win).
  • Alternative Decision: Direct demand ("Can you lower the rent?"). (Risk: Creates resistance, damages relationship).
  • Why this decision? Appreciating value shows respect. Arguing value with specific data justifies request. Conditional language creates win-win. Collaborative closing maintains relationship.
  • Resistance handled: Landlord resisted $1,600. You countered with $1,650 and 2-year commitment, creating win-win.
  • Evolution: Open → Argue Value → Condition → Close → Win-win.

💼 Dialogue 2: Negotiating Vendor Contract (Business)

Context: You're negotiating a vendor contract for software. The vendor quoted $50,000/year, but you were hoping for $40,000/year. Conversation evolves through opening → value argument → conditional language → closing.

Strategic Grammar in Context (5 C1 Patterns)

📝 Strategic Grammar Toolkit (5 C1 Patterns)

Pattern 1: Opening with "I appreciate the offer, and I can see the value"

Used to open negotiations with appreciation. Shows respect and creates positive tone.
  • "I appreciate the offer, and I can see the value."
  • "I appreciate the proposal, and I can see the value in your solution."
  • "I appreciate the property, and I can see the value."
  • "I appreciate the service, and I can see the value."
  • "I appreciate the opportunity, and I can see the value."
Common Mistake: "Can you give me a discount?" (Direct demand, creates resistance)
Native Alternative: "I appreciate the offer, and I can see the value. However, given our volume, would you be open to discussing a more competitive rate?"

Pattern 2: Arguing Value with "Given our [value argument]"

Used to argue value with specific data. Justifies request with specific data.
  • "Given our projected volume of 500 users..."
  • "Given our 2-year commitment..."
  • "Given our excellent credit history..."
  • "Given our long-term partnership..."
  • "Given our volume and commitment..."
Common Mistake: "We're a big customer." (Vague, doesn't justify request)
Native Alternative: "Given our projected volume of 500 users and willingness to commit to a 2-year contract, would you be open to discussing a more competitive pricing structure?"

Pattern 3: Conditional Language with "Would you be open to discussing"

Used to use conditional language. Creates win-win outcomes.
  • "Would you be open to discussing a more competitive rate?"
  • "Would you be willing to meet at $X?"
  • "Would you be open to discussing a more competitive pricing structure?"
  • "Would you be willing to move forward with the contract today?"
  • "Would you be open to discussing a competitive renewal rate?"
Common Mistake: "Can you give me a discount?" (Direct demand, creates resistance)
Native Alternative: "Given our volume and commitment, would you be open to discussing a more competitive pricing structure?"

Pattern 4: Handling Objections with "I can see where you're coming from"

Used to handle objections strategically. Maintains relationship and keeps negotiation open.
  • "I can see where you're coming from."
  • "I appreciate you explaining the value."
  • "I can see where you're coming from, and I appreciate you explaining the value."
  • "I can see where you're coming from. However, given our volume..."
  • "I appreciate you explaining the value. However, given our commitment..."
Common Mistake: "That's ridiculous." (Defensive, destroys relationship)
Native Alternative: "I can see where you're coming from, and I appreciate you explaining the value. However, given our volume, I was hoping we could find a middle ground."

Pattern 5: Closing with "That would be a win-win for both sides"

Used to close negotiations collaboratively. Creates win-win outcomes.
  • "That would be a win-win for both sides."
  • "That sounds reasonable."
  • "If we could agree on X, would you be willing to move forward?"
  • "That would be a win-win for both sides."
  • "Perfect. Let's move forward with..."
Common Mistake: "Can we agree on this price?" (Pushy, doesn't create win-win)
Native Alternative: "If we could agree on $42,000 per year with a 2-year contract, would you be willing to move forward with the contract today? That would be a win-win for both sides."
