Articulating Your Personal Values Clearly

Week 20 focuses on discussing what matters — values, beliefs, purpose, and meaning. Day 134 teaches you how to articulate your personal values clearly — expressing what matters to you with clarity, depth, and authenticity. B2 speakers struggle with abstract concepts or sound dogmatic. C1 speakers express their values with nuance, using hedging for beliefs and grounding statements in personal experience.

C1 Speaker: Expresses values with nuance and clarity. Says "I've been reflecting on what really matters to me lately — and I keep coming back to connection and contribution" or "I see it differently, but I'm curious what led you to that view."

Today's Philosophy

Articulating Values = Speaking from Experience + Using Hedging + Separating Person from Belief

"Articulating your personal values isn't about having all the answers. It's about speaking from experience, using hedging for beliefs (not facts), separating the person from the belief, and embracing vulnerability."

Every values discussion requires you to make five decisions simultaneously:

Experience Decision: How do I ground my values? (Theory? Experience?)
Hedging Decision: How do I express beliefs? (Facts? Beliefs?)
Separation Decision: How do I disagree? (Attack person? Separate from belief?)
Vulnerability Decision: How do I handle uncertainty? (Certainty? "I don't know"?)
Clarity Decision: How do I express values? (Vague? Clear?)

Important: Day 134 teaches you how to articulate your personal values with clarity, nuance, and philosophical depth.

The Values Articulation Loop

The 4-step process for articulating personal values clearly

1
SPEAK FROM EXPERIENCE (Ground in lived experience)
How do I ground my values? "I've found that..." / "In my experience..." (Speak from experience, not theory)
2
USE HEDGING (Hedge for beliefs, not facts)
How do I express beliefs? "I believe..." / "It seems to me..." / "In my experience..." (Use hedging for beliefs)
3
SEPARATE PERSON FROM BELIEF (Disagree respectfully)
How do I disagree? "I see that differently" / "Disagreeing with an idea doesn't mean rejecting the person" (Separate person from belief)
4
EMBRACE VULNERABILITY (Embrace "I don't know")
How do I handle uncertainty? "I'm still figuring this out" / "I'm not sure I have the answer yet" (Embrace vulnerability)

5 Values Articulation Lenses (3-Level Examples)

Every values discussion requires five decisions. Each lens has 3 levels of examples: Simple → Natural → C1 Professional.

1

Speaking from Experience

"How do I ground my values in experience?"

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High The Experience Decision

3-Level Examples

Simple (B2) "I think honesty is important."
Natural (B2+) "In my experience, honesty builds trust."
C1 Professional "I've found that honesty — even when it's difficult — has been foundational to my most meaningful relationships. It's something I've learned through experience, not just theory."

Life Example

Discussing values with partner. Decision: Ground in experience ("I've found that honesty has been foundational...")

Business Example

Discussing company values. Decision: Professional grounding ("In my experience, transparency builds trust...")

⚖️ Trade-offs
Gain (Experience) Makes your values authentic and relatable, shows you've lived them.
Lose (Experience) May seem subjective if not balanced with broader principles.

Common Mistake

Speaking from theory only. Native speakers ground values in lived experience to show authenticity.

Reflection: Am I speaking from experience or theory?

2

Using Hedging for Beliefs

"How do I hedge for beliefs, not facts?"

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High The Hedging Decision

3-Level Examples

Simple (B2) "This is the right way."
Natural (B2+) "I believe this is important."
C1 Professional "I believe that integrity matters deeply to me, though I recognize others may prioritize different values. It seems to me that living with integrity creates a sense of inner peace."

Life Example

Discussing beliefs. Decision: Hedge appropriately ("I believe that integrity matters... though I recognize others may prioritize differently")

Business Example

Discussing ethics. Decision: Professional hedging ("It seems to me that transparency builds trust...")

⚖️ Trade-offs
Gain (Hedged) Shows humility while preserving conviction, invites dialogue.
Lose (Hedged) May seem uncertain if over-hedged.

Common Mistake

Stating beliefs as facts. Native speakers hedge beliefs to show humility while preserving conviction.

Reflection: Am I hedging beliefs or stating them as facts?

3

Separating Person from Belief

"How do I separate the person from the belief?"

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High The Separation Decision

3-Level Examples

Simple (B2) "You're wrong."
Natural (B2+) "I disagree with that idea."
C1 Professional "I see that differently, but I really value hearing your perspective. Disagreeing with an idea doesn't mean rejecting the person holding it."

Life Example

Disagreeing with friend. Decision: Separate person from belief ("I see that differently, but I really value hearing your perspective...")

Business Example

Disagreeing with colleague. Decision: Professional separation ("I see that differently, but I appreciate your perspective...")

️ Trade-offs
Gain (Separated) Maintains relationships while disagreeing, shows respect.
Lose (Separated) May seem less direct if over-separated.

Common Mistake

Attacking the person. Native speakers separate the person from the belief to maintain relationships.

Reflection: Am I separating person from belief or attacking?

3 Common Mistakes

Traps that destroy values articulation

Mistake 1: Speaking from Theory Instead of Experience

❌ "Philosophy says honesty is important."
✅ "I've found that honesty — even when difficult — has been foundational to my most meaningful relationships."
Why: Theory sounds academic. Experience shows you've lived your values.

Mistake 2: Stating Beliefs as Facts

❌ "This is the only right way."
✅ "I believe this is important, though I recognize others may prioritize different values."
Why: Facts shut down dialogue. Hedged beliefs invite conversation while preserving conviction.

Mistake 3: Attacking the Person Instead of the Belief

❌ "You're wrong for thinking that."
✅ "I see that differently, but I really value hearing your perspective."
Why: Attacking the person damages relationships. Separating person from belief maintains respect.

Task 1: Discussing Life Purpose

Context: Write a message to your partner discussing what gives your life meaning.

Requirements:
  • Use 3 collocations from this lesson
  • Use 2 experience structures (e.g., "I've found that...", "In my experience...")
  • Use 2 hedging structures (e.g., "I believe...", "It seems to me...")

"I've been reflecting on what really matters to me lately — and I keep coming back to connection and contribution. "I've been thinking about what really matters to me — and I keep coming back to connection and contribution. I've found that honesty has been foundational to my relationships. I believe integrity matters, though I recognize others may see it differently. I'm still exploring what that means. I'm still figuring this out, but I think vulnerability invites deeper dialogue."

Strategic Grammar in Context (5 C1 Patterns)

📝 Strategic Grammar Toolkit (5 C1 Patterns with explanation, examples, common mistakes, native alternatives)

Pattern 1: Speaking from Experience with "I've found that"

Used to ground values in lived experience. These structures show authenticity.
  • "I've found that honesty has been foundational."
  • "In my experience, integrity creates trust."
  • "I've found that honesty — even when difficult — has been foundational to my relationships."
  • "In my experience, living with integrity creates peace."
  • "I've found that vulnerability invites deeper dialogue."
Common Mistake: "Philosophy says honesty is important." (Theory only)
Native Alternative: "I've found that honesty has been foundational."

Pattern 2: Hedging Beliefs with "I believe that"

Used to hedge for beliefs, not facts. These structures show humility while preserving conviction.
  • "I believe that integrity matters."
  • "It seems to me that honesty builds trust."
  • "I believe that integrity matters deeply, though I recognize others may prioritize differently."
  • "It seems to me that living with integrity creates peace."
  • "I believe this aligns with my values, though I recognize others may see it differently."
Common Mistake: "This is the right way." (Stating as fact)
Native Alternative: "I believe that integrity matters deeply, though I recognize..."

Pattern 3: Separating Person from Belief with "I see that differently"

Used to disagree respectfully. These structures maintain relationships while disagreeing.
  • "I see that differently."
  • "I see that differently, but I value your perspective."
  • "I see that differently, but I really value hearing your perspective."
  • "Disagreeing with an idea doesn't mean rejecting the person holding it."
  • "I see it differently, but I appreciate your thoughts."
Common Mistake: "You're wrong." (Attacking person)
Native Alternative: "I see that differently, but I really value hearing your perspective."

Pattern 4: Embracing Vulnerability with "I'm still exploring"

Used to embrace "I don't know". These structures invite deeper dialogue.
  • "I'm still exploring what that means."
  • "I'm not sure I have the answer yet."
  • "I'm still exploring what that means in practice."
  • "I'm not sure I have the answer yet, but here's what I'm exploring..."
  • "The wisest philosophical statement is often 'I'm still figuring this out.'"
Common Mistake: "I have all the answers." (False certainty)
Native Alternative: "I'm still exploring what that means in practice."

Pattern 5: Expressing with Clarity using Abstract Nouns

Used to express values with clarity. These structures use abstract nouns effectively.
  • "Connection and contribution matter to me."
  • "Integrity creates the foundation for trust."
  • "I've been reflecting on what gives life meaning."
  • "Vulnerability invites deeper dialogue than certainty."
  • "Living with integrity creates both individual and organizational peace."
Common Mistake: "Life is what it is." (Vague/shallow)
Native Alternative: "I've been reflecting on what gives life meaning."
