Talking About Money Without Fighting

Money conversations are among the hardest to navigate — whether it's splitting a bill with friends, asking for a raise, or telling your parents you can't lend them money. This week teaches you the language functions to handle these moments with clarity and grace.

"The ability to talk about money clearly and calmly is one of the most practical communication skills you can develop — it affects every relationship in your life."
75% Life Communication
25% Professional
7 Days · 60 min/day
Financial Negotiation English

Why This Week Matters

Money Is Emotional

Money isn't just about numbers — it's about values, security, and relationships. When money conversations go wrong, it's rarely about the amount. It's about feeling disrespected, unheard, or taken advantage of.

Principle 1
Start Safe
Opening a money conversation requires emotional safety. Use softening language and acknowledge sensitivity before diving in.
Principle 2
Compare Clearly
When discussing financial options, use structured comparison language that helps others see trade-offs without feeling pressured.
Principle 3
Refuse Gracefully
Saying no to financial requests is one of the hardest social skills. Learn to refuse without damaging the relationship.
Principle 4
Negotiate Collaboratively
Good money negotiations aren't about winning — they're about finding terms both sides can live with.

Before vs. After This Week

Before: Awkward & Avoidant
"Uh, so... about the money... I was wondering if... never mind, it's fine." (Changes subject, creates tension)
After: Clear & Graceful
"I understand this is a sensitive topic. I want to be upfront — I'm not in a position to lend money right now, but I'd love to help in other ways." (Honest, respectful, relationship-preserving)

Language Coverage

This week's linguistic focus — the grammar, vocabulary, and discourse patterns you'll master.

Grammar
Conditionals for hypotheticals, comparatives for options, polite refusal structures
Vocabulary
Budget, loan, expense, investment, risk, return, negotiate, terms, compromise
Discourse
Comparing options, recommending, refusing politely, negotiating sequences
Pronunciation
Numbers, currencies, stress on key financial terms, calm confident tone
Register
Talking to parents vs banker vs boss — adapting tone for each context

Quick Self-Check

Before you start, test your intuition about money conversations.

1. A friend asks to borrow $500. What's the most graceful way to refuse?

A. "I don't lend money to anyone."
B. "I understand this is hard. I'm not in a position to lend right now, but I'd love to help in other ways."
C. "Let me think about it..." (and never respond)

2. You're discussing a raise with your boss. Which approach is most effective?

A. "I need more money because I work hard."
B. "Based on my contributions this year, I'd like to discuss adjusting my compensation."
C. "Everyone else makes more than me."

3. When comparing two financial options, what language structure is most helpful?

A. "Both are fine, choose whatever."
B. "Option A has lower risk but lower return, while Option B offers higher growth with more volatility."
C. "Just go with the cheaper one."
