Talking About Habits You Want to Change

Day 8 taught you to describe your typical day in detail. Day 9 teaches you how to talk about habits you want to change — breaking bad habits, forming new ones, and discussing gradual progress. B2 speakers say "I want to stop smoking." C1 speakers use nuanced language to discuss the process of change, acknowledging difficulty and gradual progress.

C1 Insight
B2 Speaker: Says "I want to stop eating junk food" or "I want to exercise more." Uses simple, direct language that doesn't acknowledge the difficulty of change.

C1 Speaker: Uses nuanced language to discuss the process of change. Says "I'm trying to cut down on junk food, but I tend to crave it when I'm stressed" or "I'm gradually weaning myself off coffee."

Today's Philosophy

Talking About Habits = Acknowledging Struggle + Identifying Triggers + Expressing Progress + Seeking Support

"Talking about habits you want to change isn't about stating goals. It's about acknowledging the struggle, identifying triggers, expressing gradual progress, and seeking support while using nuanced language."

Every habit change conversation requires you to make five decisions simultaneously:

Acknowledgment Decision: How do I acknowledge the struggle? (Direct? Nuanced?)
Trigger Decision: How do I identify triggers? (Vague? Specific?)
Progress Decision: How do I express progress? (Binary? Gradual?)
Support Decision: How do I seek support? (Direct? Nuanced?)
Setback Decision: How do I discuss setbacks? (Negative? Realistic?)

Important: Day 9 teaches you how to talk about habits you want to change with nuance, realism, and gradual progress language.

The Habit Change Loop

The 5-step process for talking about habits you want to change

1
ACKNOWLEDGE (Acknowledge the struggle)
How do I acknowledge? "I'm struggling with..." / "I'm trying to cut down on..." (Acknowledge nuance)
2
IDENTIFY TRIGGERS (Identify triggers)
How do I identify? "I tend to... when..." / "I have a habit of..." (Identify specifically)
3
EXPRESS PROGRESS (Express gradual progress)
How do I express? "I'm making headway with..." / "I'm gradually weaning myself off..." (Express gradually)
4
SEEK SUPPORT (Seek support)
How do I seek? "I could use some accountability..." / "I'd appreciate it if you could..." (Seek nuanced)
5
DISCUSS SETBACKS (Discuss setbacks realistically)
How do I discuss? "I slipped up..." / "I fell back into old habits..." (Discuss realistically)

3 Common Mistakes

Traps that destroy habit change conversations

Mistake 1: Stating Goals Without Acknowledging Difficulty

❌ "I want to stop eating junk food."
✅ "I'm trying to cut down on junk food, but I find it really challenging when I'm stressed."
Why: Stating goals without acknowledging difficulty shows lack of self-awareness. Acknowledging difficulty shows realism.

Mistake 2: Using "I used to" Incorrectly

❌ "I used to eat junk food, but now I don't." (When you still do sometimes)
✅ "I'm trying to cut down on junk food, but I still slip up sometimes."
Why: "I used to" implies you completely stopped. "I'm trying to cut down on" shows gradual progress.

Mistake 3: Not Identifying Triggers Specifically

❌ "I eat junk food when I'm stressed."
✅ "I have a habit of reaching for junk food when I'm feeling overwhelmed at work."
Why: Identifying triggers vaguely doesn't help find solutions. Identifying specifically helps find solutions.

Friend: "That's great! It's all about progress, not perfection."

You: "Exactly! I'm trying to be more realistic about it. I'm making headway with it, and I'm gradually weaning myself off junk food."

Micro Writing: Habit Change Messages (with Model Answers)

✍️ 4 Writing Tasks with Model Answers

Task 1: Cutting Down on Coffee

Context: You're trying to cut down on coffee. Write a message to a friend acknowledging the struggle, identifying triggers, expressing gradual progress, and seeking support.

Requirements:
  • Use 3 collocations from this lesson
  • Use 2 nuanced structures (e.g., "I'm trying to cut down on...", "I'm gradually weaning myself off...")
  • Use 2 trigger structures (e.g., "I tend to...", "I have a habit of...")
  • Length: 100-150 words
Model Answer (C1)

"I'm trying to cut down on coffee, but I find it really challenging when I'm tired. I have a habit of reaching for coffee when I'm feeling overwhelmed at work. I'm making headway with it, and I'm gradually weaning myself off coffee. I could use some accountability with this, and I'd appreciate it if you could check in with me. I slipped up yesterday and fell back into old habits, but I'm getting back on track. I'm trying to be more mindful about it."

Language Breakdown
  • Nuanced: "I'm trying to cut down on...", "I'm gradually weaning myself off..."
  • Collocations: "make headway with", "wean myself off", "fall back into old habits"
  • Trigger: "I tend to...", "I have a habit of..."
  • Cohesion markers: "but", "and", "but"
Alternative Version (Casual)

"I'm trying to cut down on coffee, but I find it really challenging when I'm tired. I have a habit of reaching for coffee when I'm feeling overwhelmed at work. I'm making headway with it, and I'm gradually weaning myself off coffee. I could use some accountability with this, and I'd appreciate it if you could check in with me. I slipped up yesterday and fell back into old habits, but I'm getting back on track."

🏠 Dialogue 1: Trying to Eat Healthier (Life)

Context: You're talking to a friend about your goal to eat healthier. Conversation evolves through acknowledging struggle → identifying triggers → expressing progress → seeking support → discussing setbacks.
You:
"I'm trying to cut down on junk food, but I find it really challenging when I'm stressed. I have a habit of reaching for junk food when I'm feeling overwhelmed at work." Acknowledge
Friend:
"I understand. It's hard to break old habits."
You:
"Yeah, but I'm making headway with it, and I'm gradually weaning myself off junk food. I could use some accountability with this, and I'd appreciate it if you could check in with me." Identify Triggers
Friend:
"Of course! I'd be happy to help."
You:
"Thanks! I slipped up yesterday and fell back into old habits, but I'm getting back on track. I'm trying to be more mindful about it." Express Progress
Friend:
"That's great! It's all about progress, not perfection."
You:
"Exactly! I'm trying to be more realistic about it. I'm making headway with it, and I'm gradually weaning myself off junk food." Seek Support

Decision Point Analysis

  • Decision: Combined Acknowledge (nuanced acknowledgment) + Identify Triggers (specific trigger identification) + Express Progress (gradual progress expression) + Seek Support (nuanced support seeking) + Discuss Setbacks (realistic setback discussion).

  • Why this decision? Acknowledging struggle shows self-awareness. Identifying triggers helps find solutions. Expressing gradual progress shows realism. Seeking support shows you value it. Discussing setbacks shows resilience.
  • Resistance handled: Friend's understanding. You acknowledged struggle, identified triggers, expressed progress, sought support, and discussed setbacks.
  • Evolution: Goal setting → Acknowledge struggle → Identify triggers → Express progress → Seek support → Discuss setbacks → Realistic progress.

Strategic Grammar in Context (5 C1 Patterns)

📝 Strategic Grammar Toolkit (5 C1 Patterns with explanation, examples, common mistakes, native alternatives)

Pattern 1: Expressing Gradual Change with "I'm trying to cut down on"

Used to express gradual change. These structures show nuance and gradual progress.
  • "I'm trying to cut down on coffee."
  • "I'm trying to cut down on junk food."
  • "I'm trying to cut down on my screen time."
  • "I'm trying to cut down on smoking."
  • "I'm trying to cut down on drinking."
Common Mistake: "I want to stop coffee." (Too direct)
Native Alternative: "I'm trying to cut down on coffee."

Pattern 2: Identifying Triggers with "I have a habit of"

Used to identify triggers specifically. These structures show self-awareness and specific trigger identification.
  • "I have a habit of reaching for junk food when I'm stressed."
  • "I have a habit of procrastinating when I'm overwhelmed."
  • "I have a habit of scrolling on my phone when I'm bored."
  • "I have a habit of hitting snooze when I'm tired."
  • "I have a habit of snacking when I'm stressed."
Common Mistake: "I eat junk food when I'm stressed." (Too vague)
Native Alternative: "I have a habit of reaching for junk food when I'm stressed."

Pattern 3: Expressing Progress with "I'm making headway with"

Used to express gradual progress. These structures show realism and gradual progress.
  • "I'm making headway with cutting down on coffee."
  • "I'm making headway with my exercise routine."
  • "I'm making headway with reducing my screen time."
  • "I'm making headway with quitting smoking."
  • "I'm making headway with my morning routine."
Common Mistake: "I'm doing better." (Too binary)
Native Alternative: "I'm making headway with cutting down on coffee."

Pattern 4: Seeking Support with "I could use some accountability"

Used to seek support nuancedly. These structures show you value their support nuancedly.
  • "I could use some accountability with this."
  • "I could use some accountability with my exercise routine."
  • "I could use some accountability with reducing my screen time."
  • "I could use some accountability with quitting smoking."
  • "I could use some accountability with my morning routine."
Common Mistake: "Can you help me?" (Too direct)
Native Alternative: "I could use some accountability with this."

Pattern 5: Discussing Setbacks with "I slipped up"

Used to discuss setbacks realistically. These structures show honesty and resilience.
  • "I slipped up yesterday and fell back into old habits."
  • "I slipped up and ate junk food."
  • "I slipped up and skipped my workout."
  • "I slipped up and scrolled on my phone."
  • "I slipped up and hit snooze."
Common Mistake: "I failed." (Too negative)
Native Alternative: "I slipped up yesterday and fell back into old habits."
