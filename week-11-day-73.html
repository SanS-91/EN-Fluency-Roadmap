Delivering Constructive Feedback

Day 57-72 taught you to negotiate and handle conflict. Today, you learn how to deliver constructive feedback that drives improvement without damaging relationships. B2 speakers criticize personally. C1 speakers focus on behavior, impact, and actionable next steps.

SBI Model + Softening + Action
90 minutes
Advanced Communication Day 3 of 7
💡 C1 Insight
C1 Insight
B2 Speaker: Gives vague or personal feedback ("You're always late." / "You're not good at this."). Creates defensiveness and damages relationships.

C1 Speaker: Uses the SBI Model (Situation-Behavior-Impact). Focuses on observable behavior, specific impact, and actionable requests. Drives improvement while preserving relationships.

"B2 speakers criticize the person. C1 speakers address the behavior. The difference isn't honesty — it's delivery."
"Constructive feedback isn't about being nice. It's about being clear, specific, and focused on improvement."

Today's Philosophy

Constructive Feedback = Observation + Impact + Action

"Delivering feedback isn't about winning arguments or proving points. It's about creating clarity, driving improvement, and preserving relationships through clear, specific, and actionable communication."

Every constructive feedback conversation requires you to make five decisions simultaneously:

Timing Decision: When is the right time to give feedback? (Immediate? Scheduled?)
Specificity Decision: How specific should I be? (Vague? Specific? Overly detailed?)
Tone Decision: How should I deliver it? (Harsh? Soft? Balanced?)
Focus Decision: Am I focusing on behavior or personality? (Behavior? Personality?)
Follow-up Decision: How do I ensure follow-through? (Check-in? Support?)

Important: Day 73 teaches you the SBI Model for delivering constructive feedback that drives improvement without damaging relationships.

The SBI Feedback Loop

The 3-step SBI model that turns criticism into constructive feedback

1
SITUATION (Set the context)
When and where did it happen? "In yesterday's meeting..." (Set clear context)
2
BEHAVIOR (Describe what you observed)
What exactly did they do? "You interrupted three times..." (Describe observable behavior)
3
IMPACT (Explain the impact)
What was the result? "It made the team feel unheard..." (Explain specific impact)

5 Feedback Lenses

Every constructive feedback conversation requires five decisions. These are your 5 lenses:

1

Timing (When should I give feedback?)

"When is the right time to deliver feedback?"

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High The Timing Decision

Decision Cue

  • Immediate → Right after the event (when emotions are low)
  • Scheduled → Schedule a private meeting (for serious issues)
  • Delayed → Wait until emotions cool (for heated situations)

Life Example

Friend was late. Decision: Immediate ("Can we talk about what happened this morning?")

Business Example

Colleague interrupted in meeting. Decision: Scheduled ("Can we schedule 10 minutes to discuss yesterday's meeting?")

Register Progression

Casual "Can we talk about what happened?"
☕ Neutral "Can we discuss what happened earlier?"
💼 Professional "I'd like to discuss what happened in yesterday's meeting."
📋 Formal "I'd like to schedule a brief discussion regarding yesterday's meeting."
⚖️ Trade-offs
Gain (Immediate) Fresh in memory, prevents escalation.
Lose (Immediate) May catch them off guard.

Common Mistake

Waiting too long (feedback loses relevance) or giving it in public (creates embarrassment).

Reflection: Am I giving feedback at the right time and place?

2

Specificity (How specific should I be?)

"How specific should my feedback be?"

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High The Specificity Decision

Decision Cue

  • Vague → "You're always late." (Vague, unhelpful)
  • Specific → "You've been late three times this week." (Specific, actionable)
  • Overly detailed → Listing every single instance (Overwhelming)

Life Example

Friend is consistently late. Decision: Specific ("You've been late three times this week.")

Business Example

Colleague misses deadlines. Decision: Specific ("You've missed the last three deadlines.")

Register Progression

🏠 Casual "You've been late a few times."
☕ Neutral "You've been late three times this week."
💼 Professional "I've noticed you've missed the last three deadlines."
📋 Formal "I've observed that the last three deliverables were submitted past the deadline."
⚖️ Trade-offs
Gain (Specific) Clear, actionable, prevents misunderstanding.
Lose (Specific) May feel confrontational if not delivered carefully.

Common Mistake

Using vague language ("You're always...") instead of specific observations ("You've done X three times").

Reflection: Am I being specific or vague?

3

Tone (How should I deliver it?)

"How should I deliver the feedback?"

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High The Tone Decision

Decision Cue

  • Harsh → "You need to fix this." (Harsh, creates defensiveness)
  • Balanced → "I've noticed... and it's impacting..." (Balanced, clear)
  • Too soft → "Maybe you could maybe..." (Too soft, unclear)

Life Example

Friend is late. Decision: Balanced ("I've noticed you've been late, and it's impacting our plans.")

Business Example

Colleague misses deadlines. Decision: Balanced ("I've noticed missed deadlines, and it's impacting the team's workflow.")

Register Progression

🏠 Casual "I've noticed you've been late, and it's been tough on me."
☕ Neutral "I've noticed you've been late, and it's impacting our schedule."
💼 Professional "I've noticed the missed deadlines, and it's impacting the team's workflow."
📋 Formal "I've observed the pattern of missed deadlines, and it's impacting project timelines."
⚖️ Trade-offs
Gain (Balanced) Clear, respectful, drives improvement.
Lose (Balanced) May feel too direct for some cultures.

Common Mistake

Being too harsh (creates defensiveness) or too soft (feedback is ignored).

Reflection: Am I delivering feedback with a balanced tone?

4

Focus (Behavior vs Personality)

"Am I focusing on behavior or personality?"

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High The Focus Decision

Decision Cue

  • Behavior → "You interrupted three times." (Observable, changeable)
  • Personality → "You're so rude." (Unobservable, unchangeable)
  • Mixed → "You're rude and you interrupted." (Mixed, confusing)

Life Example

Friend interrupts. Decision: Behavior ("You interrupted three times during our conversation.")

Business Example

Colleague misses deadlines. Decision: Behavior ("You've missed the last three deadlines.")

Register Progression

🏠 Casual "You interrupted a few times."
☕ Neutral "You interrupted three times during the meeting."
💼 Professional "I noticed you interrupted three times during yesterday's meeting."
📋 Formal "I observed that you interrupted the speaker three times during yesterday's meeting."
⚖️ Trade-offs
Gain (Behavior) Observable, changeable, non-defensive.
Lose (Behavior) May feel too clinical for personal relationships.

Common Mistake

Focusing on personality ("You're lazy") instead of behavior ("You missed three deadlines").

Reflection: Am I focusing on behavior or personality?

5

Follow-up (How do I ensure follow-through?)

"How do I ensure the feedback leads to improvement?"

⭐⭐⭐⭐ High The Follow-up Decision

Decision Cue

  • Check-in → "Let's check in next week." (Supportive, clear)
  • Ignore → Give feedback and walk away (Ineffective)
  • Micro-manage → "I'll check every day." (Overbearing)

Life Example

Friend is late. Decision: Check-in ("Let's check in next week to see how it's going.")

Business Example

Colleague misses deadlines. Decision: Check-in ("Let's check in next Friday to review progress.")

Register Progression

🏠 Casual "Let's check in next week."
☕ Neutral "Let's check in next week to see how it's going."
💼 Professional "Let's schedule a follow-up next week to review progress."
📋 Formal "Let's schedule a follow-up meeting next week to review progress and next steps."
⚖️ Trade-offs
Gain (Check-in) Ensures follow-through, shows support.
Lose (Check-in) May feel like extra work.

Common Mistake

Giving feedback without follow-up (feedback is forgotten) or over-following up (feels like micromanagement).

Reflection: Am I following up effectively?

3 Common Mistakes

Traps that destroy constructive feedback

Mistake 1: Focusing on Personality

❌ "You're so lazy."
✅ "I've noticed you've missed the last three deadlines."
Why: Personality feedback creates defensiveness. Behavior feedback drives improvement.

Mistake 2: Being Vague

❌ "You're always late."
✅ "You've been late three times this week."
Why: Vague feedback is unhelpful. Specific feedback is actionable.

Mistake 3: No Follow-up

❌ Gives feedback and walks away.
✅ "Let's check in next week to see how it's going."
Why: No follow-up means feedback is forgotten. Follow-up ensures improvement.

📖 Case Studies

🏠 Case 1: Life - Giving Feedback to a Friend Who Is Consistently Late

Context: Your friend has been late to your last three meetings. You want to give constructive feedback without damaging the friendship.

Your Decision:

  • Lens 1 (Situation): "In our last three meetings..."
  • Lens 2 (Behavior): "You've been 15-20 minutes late each time."
  • Lens 3 (Impact): "It's made me feel like my time isn't valued."
  • Lens 4 (Request): "Could you aim to be on time going forward?"
  • Lens 5 (Follow-up): "Let's check in next week to see how it's going."

🎯 BREAKDOWN (Deep Analysis)

"In our last three meetings, you've been 15-20 minutes late each time."
🎯 Communication Goal: Set clear situation and describe specific behavior. 🧠 Hidden Message: "I'm not attacking you. I'm stating observable facts." ⚖️ Trade-off: Takes more time, but prevents misunderstanding. 🔄 Better Alternative? "You're always late." (Vague, creates defensiveness). ⭐ Why This Wins: Specific situation and behavior. Clear, non-defensive.
"It's made me feel like my time isn't valued."
🎯 Communication Goal: Explain specific impact using "I" statements. 🧠 Hidden Message: "This is how it impacts me, not that you're a bad person." ️ Trade-off: Takes vulnerability, but builds understanding. 🔄 Better Alternative? "You don't respect my time." (Personal attack, creates defensiveness). ⭐ Why This Wins: "I" statements explain impact without attacking personality.
"Could you aim to be on time going forward?"
Communication Goal: Make clear, actionable request. 🧠 Hidden Message: "I'm asking for change, not demanding perfection." ️ Trade-off: Takes clarity, but drives improvement. 🔄 Better Alternative? "You need to be on time." (Demanding, creates resistance). ⭐ Why This Wins: Clear, actionable request. Drives improvement.

Integrated Dialogues (Long Format - 14+ turns)

🎭 Integrated Dialogues

🏠 Dialogue 1: Giving Feedback to a Friend Who Is Consistently Late (Life)

Context: Your friend has been late to your last three meetings. You want to give constructive feedback using the SBI model.
You:
"Hey, can we talk about something?" Situation
Friend:
"Sure. What's up?"
You:
"In our last three meetings, you've been 15-20 minutes late each time." Behavior
Friend:
"Oh, I'm sorry. Traffic has been bad."
You:
"I understand. It's made me feel like my time isn't valued, and it's delayed our plans." Impact
Friend:
"I didn't realize it was impacting you like that."
You:
"Could you aim to be on time going forward? Or let me know if you're running late?" Request
Friend:
"Yeah, I can definitely do that. I'll make sure to be on time or text you."
You:
"Thanks. Let's check in next week to see how it's going." Follow-up

Decision Point Analysis

  • Decision: Combined SBI (Situation + Behavior + Impact) + Request + Follow-up.
  • Alternative Decision: "You're always late." (Risk: Creates defensiveness, damages relationship).
  • Why this decision? SBI focuses on behavior and impact, not personality. Drives improvement while preserving relationship.
  • Resistance handled: Friend was defensive. You used SBI to keep it constructive.

Morning: Give feedback to friend who is late. Afternoon: Give feedback to colleague who misses deadlines.
Morning (Life - SBI + Request + Follow-up):
"In our last three meetings, you've been late. It's made me feel like my time isn't valued. Could you aim to be on time? Let's check in next week."
Afternoon (Business - SBI + Request + Follow-up):
"On the last three projects, deliverables were late. It's impacted the team's workflow. Could you aim to submit by the deadline? Let's check in next Friday."
Reflection:
How did your feedback change between the two? Life = personal, warm. Business = professional, clear.

Flashcard Review

Situation: You need to give feedback to someone

Wrong Instinct: "You're always late."

C1 Language: Situation → Behavior → Impact → Request → Follow-up.

Trade-off: Takes more time upfront, but drives improvement while preserving relationship.

💡 Tip: In feedback, situation sets context. Behavior describes facts. Impact explains impact. Request drives improvement. Follow-up ensures follow-through.
