Making Sense of the News

We live in an age of information overload — and misinformation. This week teaches you the language of critical analysis: how to share news responsibly, question sources intelligently, express opinions with evidence, and disagree thoughtfully without turning conversations into arguments.

"The most valuable skill in the information age isn't knowing what's true — it's knowing how to talk about what might not be."

15% Professional

Critical Analysis English

Why This Week Matters

Critical Thinking Is a Communication Skill

Most people can read the news — but few can discuss it well. They either share everything uncritically, or dismiss everything as fake. This week builds the middle ground: the language of thoughtful engagement with information, where you can question without cynicism and disagree without hostility.

Principle 1
Separate Fact from Opinion
"The article says..." vs "I think..." — learn to signal clearly when you're reporting vs interpreting.
Principle 2
Check Sources Before Sharing
"I saw this somewhere" isn't enough. Learn to say "According to [source]..." and "I haven't verified this yet."
Principle 3
Express Opinions with Evidence
Move beyond "I feel like..." to "Here's what I've observed..." — backing opinions with specifics.
Principle 4
Disagree Without Dismissing
"I see it differently because..." beats "You're wrong." Learn to hold space for multiple perspectives.
Before vs. After This Week
Before: Reactive & Polarized
"Did you see this?? It's crazy!" (shares without context) · "That's fake news." (dismisses without evidence) · "You clearly don't understand." (shuts down conversation)
After: Thoughtful & Nuanced
"I came across this article from [source] — I haven't fully verified it, but it raises an interesting point about..." · "I see it differently, and here's why..." · "That's a fair point. Can you tell me more about where you're coming from?"

Language Coverage

This week's linguistic focus — the grammar, vocabulary, and discourse patterns you'll master.

Grammar
Hedging for opinions ("It seems that...", "It could be argued that..."), attribution structures ("According to...", "The report suggests..."), conditionals for analysis ("If this is true, then...")
Vocabulary
Source, evidence, bias, misinformation, sensationalism, perspective, credible, verify, claim, narrative, discourse
Discourse
Critical analysis, respectful disagreement, evidence-based argument, source attribution, balanced discussion
Pronunciation
Measured tone, stress on key evidence, calm intonation, strategic pauses for emphasis
Register
Family dinner vs professional discussion vs public debate — adjusting depth and formality

Quick Self-Check

Before you start, test your intuition about critical news discussion.

1. A family member shares a sensational news story on the group chat. What's the most thoughtful response?

A. "That's fake news. Stop sharing this stuff."
B. "Interesting — I saw a different take on this from [source]. I haven't fully verified either yet, but it's worth looking at both sides."
C. Ignore it completely.

2. Your colleague shares a strong political opinion you disagree with. How do you respond?

A. "You're clearly misinformed."
B. Nod and change the subject immediately.
C. "I see it differently, and here's why... But I'm curious — what led you to that view?"

3. You're not sure if a viral story is true. What's the responsible thing to say?

A. Share it anyway — "let people decide for themselves."
B. "I came across this but I haven't verified the source yet — I'd hold off on sharing until we know more."
C. "This is definitely true, I saw it on three different sites."
