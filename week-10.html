Talking About Health with Confidence

Health conversations require a delicate balance: clinical precision when talking to doctors, and deep empathy when comforting loved ones. This week teaches you the language functions to navigate both with confidence.

"How you talk about health affects the care you receive, the comfort you can offer, and the boundaries you set around your own well-being."
80% Life Communication
20% Professional
7 Days · 60 min/day
Health & Symptom Description English

Why This Week Matters

Health Is Vulnerable

When we're sick or worried, our vocabulary often shrinks to "I don't feel good." But precise language gets better medical care. Similarly, when someone we love is suffering, clichés like "everything will be fine" can feel dismissive. This week builds your capacity for both precision and empathy.

Principle 1
Be Precise with Professionals
Doctors need specifics: duration, intensity, location, and triggers. Learn the structured language of symptom description.
Principle 2
Be Empathetic with Loved Ones
Avoid toxic positivity. Learn to validate suffering without rushing to fix it or minimize their experience.
Principle 3
Set Wellness Boundaries
Health includes mental health. Learn to discuss burnout, stress, and limits clearly at work and at home.
Principle 4
Navigate the System
Insurance, appointments, and medical bureaucracy require a specific register. Learn to advocate for yourself effectively.

Before vs. After This Week

Before: Vague & Dismissive
To doctor: "My stomach hurts a lot." To sick friend: "Don't worry, you'll be fine soon!" (Lacks detail, minimizes feelings)
After: Precise & Validating
To doctor: "I've been experiencing a sharp pain in my lower abdomen for three days, especially after eating." To sick friend: "I'm so sorry you're going through this. I'm here for you." (Clear, actionable, deeply empathetic)

Language Coverage

This week's linguistic focus — the grammar, vocabulary, and discourse patterns you'll master.

Grammar
Present perfect for ongoing symptoms, modals for advice (should, ought to, had better), conditionals for hypothetical health scenarios
Vocabulary
Symptoms, diagnosis, treatment, wellness, mental health, burnout, insurance, coverage, side effects
Discourse
Describing symptoms chronologically, reassurance patterns, empathy expressions, checking understanding
Pronunciation
Medical terminology stress patterns, soft intonation for empathy, clear articulation for precision
Register
Formal/clinical with doctors vs intimate/soft with family vs professional with HR/manager

Quick Self-Check

Before you start, test your intuition about health communication.

1. A friend tells you they've been diagnosed with a chronic illness. What's the most empathetic response?

A. "Everything happens for a reason. Stay positive!"
B. "I'm so sorry you're going through this. I'm here for you — do you want to talk about it, or would you prefer a distraction?"
C. "My aunt had the same thing and she's totally fine now."

2. You're describing a headache to a doctor. Which description is most helpful?

A. "My head hurts a lot."
B. "I've had a throbbing pain behind my left eye for three days, especially when I look at screens."
C. "I think I might have a migraine, can you check?"

3. You need to take medical leave. How do you tell your manager?

A. "I'm sick and can't work for a while."
B. "I need to take medical leave starting next week for a health procedure. I've prepared a handover document for the team."
C. "I'm really stressed out and need a break."
