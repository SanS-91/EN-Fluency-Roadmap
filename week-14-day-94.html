Giving Constructive Feedback

Day 93 taught you to navigate cultural differences. Day 94 teaches you how to give constructive feedback — giving feedback that's specific, actionable, and well-received. B2 speakers are either too vague or too harsh. C1 speakers give feedback that's specific, solution-focused, and well-received.

Constructive Feedback + Specificity + Delivery
90 minutes
Learning & Growth Day 3 of 7
💡 C1 Insight
C1 Insight
B2 Speaker: Gives feedback that's either too vague ("You need to improve") or too harsh ("This is wrong"). Doesn't consider timing or delivery.

C1 Speaker: Gives feedback that's specific, actionable, and well-timed. Focuses on behavior, not person. Offers solutions, not just criticism.

"B2 speakers give feedback. C1 speakers give constructive feedback. The difference isn't what you say — it's how you say it."
"B2 speakers say 'You need to improve.' C1 speakers say 'I noticed X, and I'm wondering if we could try Y.' The difference isn't what you say — it's how you say it."

Today's Philosophy

Constructive Feedback = Specific + Actionable + Well-timed

"Giving constructive feedback isn't about being nice. It's about being specific about the behavior, actionable about the solution, and mindful of timing and delivery."

Every constructive feedback conversation requires you to make five decisions simultaneously:

Specificity Decision: How specific am I? (Vague? Specific?)
Focus Decision: What do I focus on? (Person? Behavior?)
Solution Decision: Do I offer solutions? (Criticism only? Solutions?)
Timing Decision: When do I give feedback? (Now? Later?)
Delivery Decision: How do I deliver it? (Harsh? Constructive?)

Important: Day 94 teaches you how to give constructive feedback that's specific, actionable, and well-received.

The Feedback Loop

The 3-step process for giving constructive feedback

1
OBSERVE (Describe specific behavior)
How do I observe? "I noticed that..." / "I've observed that..." (Describe specific behavior, not person)
2
DESCRIBE (Describe impact)
How do I describe impact? "The impact is..." / "The effect is..." (Describe impact, not judgment)
3
SUGGEST (Suggest solutions)
How do I suggest? "I'm wondering if..." / "I'm wondering if we could try..." (Suggest solutions, not demands)

5 Feedback Lenses (3-Level Examples)

Every constructive feedback conversation requires five decisions. Each lens has 3 levels of examples: Simple → Natural → C1 Professional.

1

Being Specific

"How specific am I about the behavior?"

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High The Specificity Decision

3-Level Examples

Simple (B2) "You need to improve your communication."
Natural (B2+) "I've noticed your emails could be clearer."
C1 Professional "I've noticed that in your last three emails, the main point wasn't clear until the third paragraph. I'm wondering if we could try putting the key takeaway in the first paragraph."

Life Example

Friend always late. Decision: Specific behavior ("I've noticed you've been 15-20 minutes late to our last three meetings...")

Business Example

Team member's reports unclear. Decision: Specific behavior ("I've noticed in your last three reports, the executive summary wasn't clear...")

⚖️ Trade-offs
Gain (Specific) Clear, actionable, easier to improve.
Lose (Specific) May feel too detailed if not balanced with big picture.

Common Mistake

Being vague ("You need to improve"). Native C1 speakers are specific about behavior.

Reflection: Am I being specific about behavior or vague?

2

Focusing on Behavior

"Do I focus on behavior or person?"

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High The Focus Decision

3-Level Examples

Simple (B2) "You're not a team player."
Natural (B2+) "You don't collaborate enough."
C1 Professional "I've noticed that in our last three team meetings, you haven't shared your ideas. I'm wondering if there's a reason you're holding back."

Life Example

Friend doesn't listen. Decision: Focus on behavior ("I've noticed that when I'm talking, you're often on your phone...")

Business Example

Team member interrupts. Decision: Focus on behavior ("I've noticed in our meetings, you often interrupt others...")

⚖️ Trade-offs
Gain (Behavior-focused) Less defensive, more actionable.
Lose (Behavior-focused) May feel too indirect if person needs direct feedback.

Common Mistake

Focusing on person ("You're lazy"). Native C1 speakers focus on behavior.

Reflection: Am I focusing on behavior or person?

3

Offering Solutions

"Do I offer solutions or just criticism?"

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High The Solution Decision

3-Level Examples

Simple (B2) "You need to fix this."
Natural (B2+) "You should try doing it differently."
C1 Professional "I'm wondering if we could try a different approach. What if we tried [specific solution]? What do you think?"

Life Example

Friend always late. Decision: Offer solution ("I'm wondering if we could try setting a buffer time...")

Business Example

Team member misses deadlines. Decision: Offer solution ("I'm wondering if we could try setting interim deadlines...")

⚖️ Trade-offs
Gain (Solutions) Actionable, collaborative, empowering.
Lose (Solutions) May seem presumptuous if you don't know their situation.

Common Mistake

Only criticism without solutions. Native C1 speakers offer solutions.

Reflection: Am I offering solutions or just criticism?

4

Timing

"When do I give feedback?"

⭐⭐⭐⭐ High The Timing Decision

3-Level Examples

Simple (B2) Give feedback immediately, even in front of others.
Natural (B2+) Wait for private moment, but not too long.
C1 Professional "Hey, do you have a few minutes to chat? I'd love to get your thoughts on something I've noticed."

Life Example

Friend's behavior issue. Decision: Private timing ("Hey, do you have a few minutes to chat privately?")

Business Example

Team member's performance. Decision: Private 1-on-1 ("Do you have 15 minutes for a quick chat?")

⚖️ Trade-offs
Gain (Good timing) More receptive, less defensive.
Lose (Good timing) May delay feedback too long.

Common Mistake

Wrong timing (public or too late). Native C1 speakers choose timing carefully.

Reflection: Am I choosing the right timing?

5

Delivery

"How do I deliver the feedback?"

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High The Delivery Decision

3-Level Examples

Simple (B2) "You need to improve this."
Natural (B2+) "I think you could improve this."
C1 Professional "I've noticed X, and I'm wondering if we could try Y. What are your thoughts on that?"

Life Example

Friend's behavior. Decision: Collaborative delivery ("I've noticed X, and I'm wondering if we could try Y...")

Business Example

Team member's work. Decision: Collaborative delivery ("I've noticed X, and I'm wondering if we could try Y...")

️ Trade-offs
Gain (Collaborative) Less defensive, more open to feedback.
Lose (Collaborative) May seem too soft if direct feedback needed.

Common Mistake

Harsh delivery ("You need to fix this"). Native C1 speakers use collaborative delivery.

Reflection: Am I delivering collaboratively or harshly?

3 Common Mistakes

Traps that destroy constructive feedback

Mistake 1: Being Too Vague

❌ "You need to improve your communication."
✅ "I've noticed that in your last three emails, the main point wasn't clear until the third paragraph."
Why: Vague feedback isn't actionable. Specific feedback is actionable.

Mistake 2: Focusing on Person

❌ "You're not a team player."
✅ "I've noticed that in our last three team meetings, you haven't shared your ideas."
Why: Person-focused creates defensiveness. Behavior-focused is actionable.

Mistake 3: Wrong Timing

❌ Giving feedback in front of others or too late.
✅ "Hey, do you have a few minutes to chat privately? I'd love to get your thoughts on something."
Why: Wrong timing creates defensiveness. Right timing creates openness.

💼 Case 1: Business - Giving Feedback to Team Member

Context: You're a team lead. One of your team members, Sarah, has been submitting reports late and the quality isn't up to standard. You need to give her constructive feedback.

You: "Hey Sarah, do you have a few minutes to chat? I'd love to get your thoughts on something I've noticed." Timing

Sarah: "Sure, what's up?"

You: "I've noticed that in your last three reports, the data analysis wasn't as thorough as usual, and they were submitted after the deadline. The impact is that the team has to rush to complete the final report, and it affects our overall timeline." Specific + Impact

Sarah: "Oh, I see. I've been struggling with the data collection — it's taking longer than expected."

You: "I appreciate you sharing that. I'm wondering if we could try setting interim deadlines for the data collection phase. That way, we can catch any issues earlier. What do you think about that?" Solution + Collaborative

Sarah: "That actually sounds really helpful. I think that would really help me stay on track."

You: "Great! Let's try that for the next report. And if you run into any issues with the data collection, let me know early so we can adjust. Does that sound good?" Confirm

Sarah: "Yeah, that sounds great. Thanks for the feedback — I really appreciate it."

Micro Writing: Constructive Feedback Messages (with Model Answers)

✍️ 4 Writing Tasks with Model Answers

Task 1: Feedback to Team Member (Business)

Context: Your team member has been submitting reports late. Write a constructive feedback message.

Requirements:
  • Use 3 collocations from this lesson
  • Use 2 feedback structures (e.g., "I've noticed that...", "I'm wondering if...")
  • Use 1 solution structure (e.g., "I'm wondering if we could try...")
  • Length: 100-150 words
Model Answer (C1)

"Hey [Name], do you have a few minutes to chat? I've noticed that your last three reports were submitted after the deadline. The impact is that the team has to rush to complete the final report. I'm wondering if we could try setting interim deadlines for the data collection phase. That way, we can catch any issues earlier. What do you think about that?"

Language Breakdown
  • Observation: "I've noticed that..."
  • Impact: "The impact is..."
  • Solution: "I'm wondering if we could try..."
  • Collaborative: "What do you think about that?"
Alternative Version (More Direct)

"Hi [Name], I've noticed your last three reports were late. This affects our team timeline. Could we try setting interim deadlines? Let me know your thoughts."

Self-check Checklist
  • □ đủ word count (100-150 words)
  • □ dùng đủ 3 collocations (reports, deadline, interim deadlines)
  • □ dùng đủ 2 feedback structures (I've noticed that, I'm wondering if)
  • □ dùng đủ 1 solution structure (I'm wondering if we could try)
  • □ có opening → observation → impact → solution → closing

"Oh, I see. I've been struggling with the data collection — it's taking longer than expected."
You:
"I appreciate you sharing that. I'm wondering if we could try setting interim deadlines for the data collection phase. That way, we can catch any issues earlier. What do you think about that?" Solution + Collaborative
Sarah:
"That actually sounds really helpful. I think that would really help me stay on track."

Sarah:
"Yeah, that sounds great. Thanks for the feedback — I really appreciate it."

Decision Point Analysis

  • Decision: Combined Timing (private moment) + Observation (specific behavior) + Impact (team impact) + Solution (interim deadlines) + Confirmation (Does that sound good?).
  • Alternative Decision: Direct criticism in public. (Risk: Defensive, damages relationship).
  • Why this decision? Private timing creates safety. Specific observation is actionable. Impact shows why it matters. Solution is collaborative. Confirmation ensures agreement.
  • Resistance handled: Sarah was struggling with data collection. You offered solution (interim deadlines) to help.
  • Evolution: Timing → Observation → Impact → Solution → Confirmation.

Strategic Grammar in Context (5 C1 Patterns)

📝 Strategic Grammar Toolkit (5 C1 Patterns)

Pattern 1: Setting Up with "Do you have a few minutes?"

Used to set up private conversation for feedback. Creates safety for feedback conversation.
  • "Do you have a few minutes to chat?"
  • "Do you have a sec to talk?"
  • "Do you have a few minutes? I'd love to get your thoughts on something."
  • "Do you have a few minutes? There's something I'd love to discuss."
  • "Do you have a few minutes? I'd love to get your thoughts on something I've noticed."
Common Mistake: Giving feedback in public or without warning. (Creates defensiveness)
Native Alternative: "Do you have a few minutes to chat?"

Pattern 2: Observing with "I've noticed that..."

Used to give specific observation. Makes feedback specific and actionable.
  • "I've noticed that you've been late."
  • "I've noticed that in your last three reports..."
  • "I've noticed that you haven't been sharing ideas."
  • "I've noticed that your emails tend to be detailed."
  • "I've noticed that in our meetings, you often interrupt."
Common Mistake: "You always..." or "You never..." (Too absolute)
Native Alternative: "I've noticed that..."

Pattern 3: Impact with "The impact is..."

Used to show impact of behavior. Shows why feedback matters.
  • "The impact is that we're behind schedule."
  • "The impact is that the team has to rush."
  • "The impact is that it cuts into our time."
  • "The impact is that it takes longer to get to key points."
  • "The impact is that the audience might miss the key takeaway."
Common Mistake: Not explaining impact. (Feedback seems arbitrary)
Native Alternative: "The impact is..."

Pattern 4: Suggesting with "I'm wondering if..."

Used to suggest solutions collaboratively. Makes suggestion collaborative, not demanding.
  • "I'm wondering if we could try setting interim deadlines."
  • "I'm wondering if we could try a different approach."
  • "I'm wondering if there's a better time for you."
  • "I'm wondering if we could try putting the key takeaway first."
  • "I'm wondering if we could try a round-robin approach."
Common Mistake: "You should..." or "You need to..." (Too demanding)
Native Alternative: "I'm wondering if we could try..."

Pattern 5: Confirming with "Does that sound good?"

Used to confirm agreement. Ensures agreement and collaboration.
  • "Does that sound good?"
  • "Does that work for you?"
  • "What do you think about that?"
  • "Does that sound like a plan?"
  • "Does that work for you? Let's try that."
Common Mistake: Not confirming. (Assumes agreement)
Native Alternative: "Does that sound good?"
