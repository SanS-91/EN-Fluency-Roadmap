Today's Philosophy

Symptom Description = Precision + Structure

"Describing symptoms isn't about using complex medical terms. It's about observing carefully and communicating what you experience with precision, structure, and clarity."

Every symptom description requires you to address five dimensions:

Location: Where exactly do you feel it?
Intensity: How strong is it? (Scale 1-10)
Duration: How long has it been happening?
Quality: What does it feel like? (sharp, dull, throbbing, burning)
Triggers: What makes it better or worse?

Important: Day 57-63 taught you the competencies. Day 64 teaches you how to describe symptoms clearly so doctors can diagnose effectively. This isn't medical training — it's communication training.

5 Description Lenses

Every symptom description requires five decisions. These are your 5 lenses:

1

Location (Where exactly?)

"Where exactly do you feel it?"

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High The Location Decision

Decision Cue

  • Specific location → "It's in my lower back, on the left side"
  • General area → "It's somewhere in my back"
  • Multiple locations → "It's in my back and radiates down my leg"

Life Example

B2: "My back hurts." C1: "I have pain in my lower back, on the left side, and it radiates down my leg."

Business Example

B2: "I'm stressed." C1: "I've been experiencing tension headaches at the base of my skull for two weeks."

Register Progression

🏠 Casual "It's in my lower back"
Neutral "The pain is in my lower back"
💼 Professional "I'm experiencing pain in my lower back"
📋 Formal "I'm experiencing localized pain in my lower back region"
️ Trade-offs
Gain (Specific) Helps doctor diagnose accurately.
Lose (Specific) Requires more observation.

Common Mistake

Being vague ("It hurts") instead of specific ("It's in my lower back").

Reflection: Am I describing where exactly, or just saying "it hurts"?

2

Intensity (How strong?)

"How strong is the pain?"

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High The Intensity Decision

Decision Cue

  • Mild (1-3): "It's a mild ache"
  • Moderate (4-6): "It's moderate, about 5 out of 10"
  • Severe (7-10): "It's severe, about 8 out of 10"

Life Example

B2: "It hurts a lot." C1: "The pain is about 7 out of 10, quite severe."

Business Example

B2: "I'm very stressed." C1: "The stress level is about 8 out of 10, affecting my work."

Register Progression

🏠 Casual "It's pretty bad, about 7 out of 10"
Neutral "The pain is moderate, about 5 out of 10"
💼 Professional "On a scale of 1 to 10, I'd rate it as 7"
📋 Formal "I would characterize the pain level as severe, approximately 7 to 8"
⚖️ Trade-offs
Gain (Quantify) Helps doctor assess urgency.
Lose (Quantify) Requires self-awareness.

Common Mistake

Exaggerating ("It's the worst pain ever") or minimizing ("It's not that bad").

Reflection: Am I being honest about the severity?

3

Duration (How long?)

"How long has it been happening?"

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High The Duration Decision

Decision Cue

  • Acute: "It started three days ago"
  • Chronic: "It's been happening for three months"
  • Intermittent: "It comes and goes"

Life Example

B2: "It's been hurting for a while." C1: "It started three days ago and has been getting progressively worse."

Business Example

B2: "I've been stressed for a while." C1: "The stress started two months ago when my workload increased."

Register Progression

🏠 Casual "It started a few days ago"
☕ Neutral "It's been happening for three days"
💼 Professional "The symptoms began approximately 72 hours ago"
📋 Formal "The onset of symptoms occurred approximately 72 hours ago"
⚖️ Trade-offs
Gain (Specific) Helps diagnose acute vs chronic.
Lose (Specific) Requires careful observation.

Common Mistake

Saying "I don't know" instead of estimating the timeline.

Reflection: Do I know when it started?

4

Quality (What does it feel like?)

"What does the pain feel like?"

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High The Quality Decision

Decision Cue

  • Sharp: "It's a sharp, stabbing pain"
  • Dull: "It's a dull, aching pain"
  • Throbbing: "It's a throbbing pain"
  • Burning: "It's a burning sensation"

Life Example

B2: "It hurts." C1: "It's a sharp, shooting pain, not dull."

Business Example

B2: "I'm stressed." C1: "I'm experiencing tension headaches with pressure at the base of my skull."

Register Progression

🏠 Casual "It's a sharp pain"
Neutral "It's a sharp, stabbing pain"
💼 Professional "It's a sharp, stabbing sensation"
📋 Formal "I'm experiencing acute, stabbing pain"
⚖️ Trade-offs
Gain (Descriptive) Helps differentiate types of conditions.
Lose (Descriptive) Requires vocabulary.

Common Mistake

Using only "pain" instead of specific descriptors (sharp, dull, throbbing).

Reflection: Am I using the most accurate word?

5

Triggers (What makes it better/worse?)

"What makes it better or worse?"

⭐⭐⭐⭐ High The Trigger Decision

Decision Cue

  • Worse with: "It gets worse when I move"
  • Better with: "It gets better when I rest"
  • No pattern: "It's constant regardless of activity"

Life Example

B2: "It always hurts." C1: "It gets worse when I sit for long periods, better when I walk."

Business Example

B2: "I'm always stressed." C1: "The headaches worsen during stressful meetings, improve when I take breaks."

Register Progression

🏠 Casual "It hurts more when I move"
☕ Neutral "It gets worse with movement, better with rest"
💼 Professional "The pain intensifies with physical activity"
📋 Formal "Symptoms are exacerbated by physical exertion"
⚖️ Trade-offs
Gain (Patterns) Helps identify causes and treatments.
Lose (Patterns) Requires observation over time.

Common Mistake

Saying "I don't know" without trying to identify patterns.

Reflection: Have I paid attention to what makes it better or worse?

3 Common Mistakes

Traps that prevent accurate diagnosis

Mistake 1: Being Too Vague

❌ "I feel bad. Something's wrong."
✅ "I've been experiencing a dull headache and nausea for three days."
Why: Vague descriptions don't help doctors diagnose. Specific symptoms lead to accurate diagnosis.

Mistake 2: Minimizing Symptoms

❌ "It's not that bad. It's probably nothing."
✅ "The pain is moderate, about 5 out of 10, and it's been getting worse."
Why: Minimizing symptoms leads to underdiagnosis. Honest assessment leads to proper treatment.

Mistake 3: Using Medical Terms Incorrectly

❌ "I think I have gastritis." (Self-diagnosing)
✅ "I have stomach pain after eating, especially spicy food."
Why: Self-diagnosis can mislead doctors. Describing symptoms accurately helps them diagnose correctly.

The Symptom Description Decoder (8 Situations)

🔍 The Decision Decoder

Situation Option A (B2 Default) Option B (Safe) Option C (C1) Trade-off of C When B is better?
1. Life: Describing headache to doctor "My head hurts." "I have a headache." "I have a throbbing headache on the right side, behind my eye, for three days." Requires more vocabulary. When you're in too much pain to describe.
2. Life: Describing stomach pain "My stomach hurts." "I have stomach pain." "I have sharp pain in my lower right abdomen, especially after eating." Requires observation. When pain is severe and you need immediate help.
3. Life: Describing fatigue "I'm tired." "I'm very tired." "I've been experiencing persistent fatigue for two weeks, even after sleeping 8 hours." Requires self-awareness. When you're too exhausted to articulate.
4. Life: Describing anxiety symptoms "I'm stressed." "I'm very anxious." "I've been experiencing rapid heartbeat and difficulty breathing when stressed." Requires emotional awareness. When you're too anxious to describe symptoms.
5. Business: Describing work stress to doctor "Work is stressful." "I'm under a lot of pressure." "I've been experiencing tension headaches and insomnia due to increased workload." Requires self-reflection. When you're in crisis and need immediate help.
6. Life: Describing chronic pain "It always hurts." "It hurts most of the time." "The pain is constant, about 6 out of 10, and worsens with movement." Requires detailed observation. When pain is acute and you need immediate relief.
7. Life: Describing allergy symptoms "I have allergies." "I'm having allergic reactions." "I've been experiencing sneezing and itchy eyes after being outdoors." Requires pattern recognition. When symptoms are severe and you need medication.
8. Life: Describing sleep problems "I can't sleep." "I have insomnia." "I have difficulty falling asleep and wake up multiple times during the night." Requires sleep tracking. When you need immediate sleep aid.

Integrated Dialogues (Long Format - 14+ turns)

Integrated Dialogues

Dialogue 1: Describing Chronic Pain to a Doctor

Context: You're at the doctor's office describing back pain that's been affecting your work and sleep.
Doctor:
"What brings you in today?"
You:
"I've been experiencing back pain for about three months. It's in my lower back, on the left side, and it radiates down my leg." Location
Doctor:
"How severe is the pain?"
You:
"It's about 6 out of 10 during the day, but at night it can get to 8 out of 10. It's affecting my sleep." Intensity
Doctor:
"When did it start?"
You:
"It started about three months ago, and it's been getting progressively worse. It used to come and go, but now it's pretty constant." Duration
Doctor:
"What does the pain feel like?"
You:
"It's a sharp, shooting pain, not dull. Sometimes it feels like electric shocks down my leg." Quality
Doctor:
"What makes it better or worse?"
You:
"It gets worse when I sit for long periods or bend over. It gets better when I walk or lie down. Standing for long periods also makes it worse." Triggers
Doctor:
"Have you tried any treatments?"
You:
"I've tried ibuprofen, which helps a little. I've also tried heat therapy, which provides temporary relief."
Doctor:
"Based on your description, this sounds like it could be sciatica. Let's do some tests to confirm."

Decision Point Analysis

  • Decision: Used all 5 dimensions (Location, Intensity, Duration, Quality, Triggers) to describe symptoms precisely.
  • Alternative Decision: "My back hurts." (Risk: Vague, doesn't help doctor diagnose).
  • Why this decision? Specific description helps doctor identify potential causes (sciatica, herniated disc) and recommend appropriate tests.
  • Result: Doctor was able to identify potential condition (sciatica) based on precise description.

💼 Dialogue 2: Describing Work Stress to a Doctor

Context: You're experiencing physical symptoms from work stress and need to communicate this clearly to your doctor.
Doctor:
"What brings you in today?"

Doctor:

You:

Doctor:
"Based on your description, this sounds like stress-related tension headaches and insomnia. Let's discuss some management strategies."

Decision Point Analysis

  • Decision: Connected physical symptoms to stress trigger, described specific symptoms with location, intensity, and duration.
  • Alternative Decision: "I'm stressed." (Risk: Vague, doesn't show physical impact).
  • Why this decision? Specific description of physical symptoms helps doctor assess severity and recommend appropriate treatment.
  • Result: Doctor identified stress-related condition and can recommend targeted treatment (stress management, sleep hygiene).

Symptom Description Mastery

🎯 Mastery Checklist

I describe location precisely

I specify exact location, not just general area.

I quantify intensity

I use 1-10 scale and descriptive adjectives.

I establish timeline

I describe when it started and how it's changed.

I describe quality

I use specific descriptors (sharp, dull, throbbing).

I identify triggers

I describe what makes it better or worse.

Continuity Check: If you can consistently use all 5 dimensions to describe symptoms, you are ready for Day 65: Giving and Receiving Health Advice.

Flashcard Review

Situation: You need to describe back pain to a doctor

Wrong Instinct: "My back hurts."

C1 Decision: Location + Intensity + Duration + Quality + Triggers

Trade-off: Requires more observation and vocabulary, but leads to accurate diagnosis.

💡 Tip: C1 speakers describe what they experience. B2 speakers describe how they feel.
