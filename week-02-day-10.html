Comparing Past and Present Routines

Day 8-9 taught you to describe your typical day and talk about habits you want to change. Day 10 teaches you how to compare past and present routines — discussing how your daily life has evolved, what's changed, and what's stayed the same. B2 speakers use simple past vs present. C1 speakers use nuanced comparisons with sophisticated time expressions and reflective language.

Today's Philosophy

Comparing Routines = Establishing Timeline + Highlighting Changes + Noting Continuities + Reflecting

"Comparing past and present routines isn't about listing differences. It's about establishing a timeline, highlighting changes, noting continuities, and reflecting on what these changes reveal about your life."

Every routine comparison requires you to make five decisions simultaneously:

Timeline Decision: How do I establish the timeline? (Vague? Specific?)
Change Decision: How do I highlight changes? (Simple? Nuanced?)
Continuity Decision: How do I note what stayed the same? (Basic? Sophisticated?)
Reflection Decision: How do I reflect on changes? (Surface? Deep?)
Contrast Decision: How do I contrast past and present? (Direct? Nuanced?)

Important: Day 10 teaches you how to compare past and present routines with sophistication, reflection, and nuanced language.

The Comparison Loop

The 4-step process for comparing past and present routines

1
ESTABLISH TIMELINE (Set the timeframe)
How do I establish timeline? "Back in my university days..." / "These days..." / "When I was younger..." (Set specific timeframe)
2
HIGHLIGHT CHANGES (Show what's different)
How do I highlight changes? "I used to... whereas now I..." / "These days I tend to..." (Highlight nuanced changes)
3
NOTE CONTINUITIES (Show what's the same)
How do I note continuities? "One thing that hasn't changed is..." / "I still..." (Note sophisticated continuities)
4
REFLECT (Reflect on the changes)
How do I reflect? "This reflects a shift in..." / "I think this shows..." (Reflect deeply)

3 Common Mistakes

Traps that destroy routine comparisons

Mistake 1: Using Vague Time References

❌ "Before, I did this. Now I do that."
✅ "Back in my university days, I used to... whereas these days I tend to..."
Why: Vague references lack context. Specific timelines provide context and meaning.

Mistake 2: Only Focusing on Changes

❌ "I used to do X. Now I do Y."
✅ "I used to do X, whereas these days I do Y. One thing that hasn't changed is..."
Why: Only focusing on changes misses what's important. Noting continuities shows what matters.

Mistake 3: Surface-Level Observations

❌ "I think I'm busier now."
✅ "I think this reflects a shift in my priorities, as I've become more focused on..."
Why: Surface observations lack depth. Deep reflection shows self-awareness and insight.

🏠 Case 1: Life - Comparing University Days to Now

Context: You're talking to a friend about how your daily routine has changed since university. You need to establish timeline, highlight changes, note continuities, and reflect.

Friend: "That's interesting. What else has changed?"

You: "Well, I used to exercise every day without fail, whereas these days I tend to exercise a few times a week, which I think reflects a more balanced approach to fitness. I think this shows that I'm less focused on perfection and more focused on sustainability."

Friend: "That makes sense. What about your social life?"

You: "I used to go out every weekend, whereas these days I find myself preferring quiet nights at home, which I think shows I'm valuing quality time over quantity. One constant throughout has been my love of reading, which remains a daily ritual."

Micro Writing: Comparison Messages (with Model Answers)

✍️ 4 Writing Tasks with Model Answers

Task 1: Comparing Sleep Schedule

Context: Write about how your sleep schedule has changed over time. Establish timeline, highlight changes, note continuities, and reflect.

Requirements:
  • Use 3 time expressions from this lesson
  • Use 2 contrast structures (e.g., "I used to... whereas...")
  • Use 1 reflection structure (e.g., "This reflects...")
  • Length: 100-150 words
Model Answer (C1)

"Back in my university days, I used to wake up at 7 without fail, whereas these days I tend to wake up closer to 8, which reflects a shift in my priorities. One thing that hasn't changed is my morning coffee ritual, which remains a constant despite all the other changes. I think this shows that while my schedule has evolved, certain habits remain important to me."

Language Breakdown
  • Time expressions: "Back in my university days", "these days", "without fail"
  • Contrast: "I used to... whereas...", "despite"
  • Reflection: "which reflects", "I think this shows"
  • Continuity: "One thing that hasn't changed", "remains a constant"
Alternative Version (Casual)

"When I was in university, I used to wake up at 7, but these days I wake up closer to 8. One thing that hasn't changed is my morning coffee. I think this shows that while my schedule has changed, some things stay the same."

Context: You're talking to a friend about how your daily routine has changed since university. Conversation evolves through establishing timeline → highlighting changes → noting continuities → reflecting.
You:

Friend:

"That's really thoughtful. It sounds like you've evolved a lot."
You:
"Yeah, I think this reflects a shift in how I view work-life balance and what's truly important to me." Reflect

Strategic Grammar in Context (5 C1 Patterns)

📝 Strategic Grammar Toolkit (5 C1 Patterns with explanation, examples, common mistakes, native alternatives)

Pattern 1: Establishing Timeline with "Back in my..."

Used to establish specific timeframe. These structures provide context and specificity.
  • "Back in my university days, I used to..."
  • "When I first started my career, I used to..."
  • "When I was younger, I used to..."
  • "Back in my early twenties, I used to..."
  • "When I was in my first job, I used to..."
Common Mistake: "Before, I used to..." (Too vague)
Native Alternative: "Back in my university days, I used to..."

Pattern 2: Creating Contrast with "I used to... whereas..."

Used to create nuanced contrast. These structures show evolution with sophistication.
  • "I used to wake up at 7, whereas these days I tend to wake up at 8."
  • "I used to exercise every day, whereas these days I exercise a few times a week."
  • "I used to work 9-5, whereas these days I have flexibility."
  • "I used to go out every weekend, whereas these days I prefer quiet nights."
  • "I used to eat junk food, whereas these days I eat healthier."
Common Mistake: "I used to... but now..." (Too simple)
Native Alternative: "I used to... whereas these days..."

Pattern 3: Noting Continuity with "One thing that hasn't changed"

Used to note what's stayed the same. These structures show what remains important.
  • "One thing that hasn't changed is my morning coffee ritual."
  • "One thing that hasn't changed is my commitment to quality."
  • "One thing that hasn't changed is my love of reading."
  • "One constant throughout has been my attention to detail."
  • "One thing that remains the same is my dedication to fitness."
Common Mistake: "I still..." (Too simple)
Native Alternative: "One thing that hasn't changed is..."

Pattern 4: Reflecting with "which reflects"

Used to add reflection and meaning. These structures demonstrate self-awareness and insight.
  • "which reflects a shift in my priorities."
  • "which reflects a more balanced approach to fitness."
  • "which reflects a shift in how I view work-life balance."
  • "which I think shows I've learned to prioritize better."
  • "which I think shows I'm valuing quality time over quantity."
Common Mistake: "I think I'm..." (Surface-level)
Native Alternative: "which reflects..."

Pattern 5: Using "I find myself" for Discovery

Used to express discovery of new habits. These structures show self-awareness of gradual changes.
  • "I find myself preferring quiet nights at home."
  • "I find myself waking up later these days."
  • "I find myself being more selective about projects."
  • "I find myself valuing quality over quantity."
  • "I find myself exercising less frequently but more intentionally."
Common Mistake: "I prefer..." (Too direct)
Native Alternative: "I find myself..."
