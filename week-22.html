Crisis & Emergency Communication

When everything goes wrong at once, clarity saves lives — and relationships. This week teaches you the language of crisis: how to give calm instructions under pressure, report facts accurately, coordinate responses, and debrief afterward with care. The words you choose in a crisis define whether people panic or pull together.

"In a crisis, people don't need perfect words — they need clear ones. Calm. Specific. Direct. That's the language of leadership when everything falls apart."

Why This Week Matters

Crisis Reveals Communication Habits

When stress hits, we revert to our default communication patterns. If your default is vague, emotional, or passive, a crisis will amplify those weaknesses. This week builds the muscle memory for calm, clear, directive communication — so when it matters most, your words become anchors, not accelerants.

Principle 1
Calm Before Clear Before Complete
Your tone sets the emotional temperature. Calm first, then clear facts, then full details. Order matters.
Principle 2
Use Imperatives with Care
"Call 911" works. "Please, if you could possibly call 911" doesn't. Crisis language needs directness, softened by tone.
Principle 3
Name What You Know, Name What You Don't
"The fire is in the kitchen. I don't know if anyone is upstairs." Honesty about unknowns builds trust.
Principle 4
Debrief With Humanity, Not Just Data
After the crisis, people need to process emotions, not just review facts. Hold space for both.
Before vs. After This Week
Before: Panic or Freeze
"Oh my god, what do we do?! I don't know! Somebody help!" (Emotional, unclear, paralyzing) · Or: [frozen silence]
After: Calm, Clear, Coordinated
"Listen to me. The fire is in the kitchen. Sarah, call 911. Tom, grab the kids and go to the front yard. I'll get the dog. We'll meet outside. Go now."

Language Coverage

This week's linguistic focus — the grammar, vocabulary, and discourse patterns you'll master.

Grammar
Imperatives for instructions ("Call 911", "Stay here"), present simple for facts, modals for urgency ("must", "need to")
Vocabulary
Emergency, crisis, instruction, panic, coordinate, debrief, protocol, incident, response, evacuate
Discourse
Emergency protocols, fact-reporting sequences, debriefing structures, action-item language
Pronunciation
Clear, firm articulation; calm, controlled tone; strategic pauses; steady pacing
Register
Authoritative in crisis vs supportive in debrief vs calm in coordination

Quick Self-Check

Before you start, test your intuition about crisis communication.

1. Your child has fallen and may have broken their arm. What's the most effective first response?

A. "Oh my god, are you okay?! Don't move! Oh no, this is terrible!"
B. "I'm here. Don't move your arm. I'm going to call for help. Look at me — breathe. We'll get this handled."
C. "It's probably fine. Let's just wait and see."

2. There's a fire alarm in your office building. What's the clearest instruction to give a panicked colleague?

A. "We should probably, like, maybe leave? I'm not sure what to do."
B. "EVERYONE GET OUT NOW!!! RUN!!!"
C. "Listen to me. Leave your things. Walk calmly to the east exit. I'll walk with you. Let's go."

3. After a family emergency has passed, your partner is silent. What's the most supportive thing to say?

A. "Well, at least it's over now. Let's move on."
B. "You should talk about how you feel. It's not healthy to bottle it up."
C. "I'm here. You don't have to talk. But when you're ready, I want to hear how that felt for you."
