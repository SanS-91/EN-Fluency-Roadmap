Today's Philosophy

Tracking & Describing Habits = Identifying Triggers + Tracking Progress + Describing Routines + Maintaining Momentum

"Tracking and describing habits isn't just about doing them. It's about identifying triggers, tracking progress, describing routines, and maintaining momentum while overcoming setbacks."

Every habit tracking requires you to make five decisions simultaneously:

Trigger Decision: How do I identify triggers? (Vague? Specific?)
Progress Decision: How do I track progress? (Passive? Active?)
Routine Decision: How do I describe routines? (Simple? Detailed?)
Momentum Decision: How do I maintain momentum? (Inconsistent? Consistent?)
Setback Decision: How do I overcome setbacks? (Give up? Adapt?)

Important: Day 14 teaches you how to track and describe one habit with self-awareness, detail, and consistency.

The Habit Tracking Loop

The 4-step process for tracking and describing one habit

1
IDENTIFY TRIGGERS (Identify triggers)
How do I identify triggers? "I've noticed that when X happens, I tend to..." (Identify specifically)
2
TRACK PROGRESS (Track progress)
How do I track progress? "I've been tracking my progress and noticed..." (Track actively)
3
DESCRIBE ROUTINES (Describe routines)
How do I describe routines? "I've been making a conscious effort to..." (Describe in detail)
4
MAINTAIN MOMENTUM (Maintain momentum)
How do I maintain momentum? "I've been trying to maintain momentum by..." (Maintain consistently)

5 Habit Lenses (3-Level Examples)

Every habit tracking requires five decisions. Each lens has 3 levels of examples: Simple → Natural → C1 Professional.

1

Identifying Triggers

"How do I identify triggers specifically?"

⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Very High The Trigger Decision

3-Level Examples

Simple (B2) "I do this when I'm stressed."
Natural (B2+) "I tend to do this when I'm stressed."
C1 Professional "I've noticed that when I'm feeling stressed, I tend to reach for unhealthy snacks. I've been tracking my triggers and noticed a pattern."

Life Example

Unhealthy eating. Decision: Specific trigger identification ("I've noticed that when I'm feeling stressed...")

Business Example

Procrastination. Decision: Professional trigger identification ("I've noticed that when I have a large project...")

⚖️ Trade-offs
Gain (Specific) Helps you understand your habits, helps you change them.
Lose (Specific) Requires self-reflection, may be uncomfortable.

Common Mistake

Vague triggers. Native speakers identify triggers specifically to understand their habits.

Reflection: Am I identifying triggers specifically or vaguely?

3 Common Mistakes

Traps that destroy habit tracking

Mistake 1: Vague Triggers Instead of Specific Triggers

❌ "I do this when I'm stressed."
✅ "I've noticed that when I'm feeling stressed, I tend to reach for unhealthy snacks."
Why: Vague triggers prevent you from understanding your habits. Specific triggers help you understand and change them.

Mistake 2: Passive Tracking Instead of Active Tracking

❌ "I've been doing this for a week."
✅ "I've been tracking my progress and noticed that I've been more consistent when I set specific goals."
Why: Passive tracking doesn't help you see patterns. Active tracking helps you stay motivated and see patterns.

Mistake 3: Giving Up Instead of Adapting

"I give up when I fail."
✅ "I've noticed that when I slip up, I try to identify what triggered the setback and adjust my approach."
Why: Giving up prevents you from achieving your goals. Adapting helps you recover and stay consistent.

🏃 Case 1: Life - Building an Exercise Habit

Context: You've been building an exercise habit. You need to describe your routine, track your progress, and discuss maintaining momentum.

You: "I've been making a conscious effort to establish a morning exercise routine. I've been tracking my progress and noticed that I've been more consistent when I set specific goals. I've noticed that when I'm feeling tired, I tend to skip my workout."

Friend: "That's great! How do you maintain momentum?"

You: "I've been trying to maintain momentum by setting small, achievable goals and celebrating small wins. I've noticed that when I slip up, I try to identify what triggered the setback and adjust my approach accordingly."

Friend: "That's a great approach! How do you describe your routine?"

You: "I've been making a conscious effort to exercise for 30 minutes every morning. I've been tracking my progress and noticed I've been more consistent. I've noticed that when I'm feeling motivated, I tend to exercise longer."

Friend: "That's great! How do you overcome setbacks?"

You: "I've noticed that when I slip up, I try to identify what triggered the setback and adjust my approach accordingly. I've been trying to maintain momentum by setting small, achievable goals."

Micro Writing: Habit Messages (with Model Answers)

✍️ 4 Writing Tasks with Model Answers

Task 1: Describe a New Habit

Context: You've been building a new habit. Write a message describing your new habit.

Requirements:
  • Use 3 collocations from this lesson
  • Use 2 routine structures (e.g., "I've been making a conscious effort to...", "I've been tracking my progress...")
  • Use 2 momentum structures (e.g., "I've been trying to maintain momentum by...", "I've noticed that when...")
  • Length: 100-150 words
Model Answer (C1)

"I've been making a conscious effort to establish a morning exercise routine. I've been tracking my progress and noticed that I've been more consistent when I set specific goals. I've been trying to maintain momentum by setting small, achievable goals and celebrating small wins. I've noticed that when I slip up, I try to identify what triggered the setback and adjust my approach accordingly."

Language Breakdown
  • Routine: "I've been making a conscious effort to...", "I've been tracking my progress..."
  • Collocations: "conscious effort", "tracking my progress", "maintain momentum"
  • Momentum: "I've been trying to maintain momentum by...", "I've noticed that when..."
  • Cohesion markers: "I've been", "I've noticed", "I've been trying"
Alternative Version (Casual)

"I've been trying to build a morning exercise routine. I've been tracking my progress and noticed I've been more consistent. I've been trying to maintain momentum by setting small goals. I've noticed that when I slip up, I try to get back on track."

Self-check Checklist
  • □ đủ word count (100-150 words)
  • □ dùng đủ 3 collocations (conscious effort, tracking my progress, maintain momentum)
  • □ dùng đủ 2 routine structures (I've been making a conscious effort to, I've been tracking my progress)
  • □ dùng đủ 2 momentum structures (I've been trying to maintain momentum by, I've noticed that when)
  • □ có opening → development → closing

Decision Point Analysis

  • Decision: Combined Identify Triggers (specific trigger identification) + Track Progress (active progress tracking) + Describe Routines (detailed routine description) + Maintain Momentum (consistent momentum).

  • Why this decision? Identifying triggers helps you understand your habits. Tracking progress helps you stay motivated. Describing routines helps you stay consistent. Maintaining momentum helps you achieve your goals.
  • Resistance handled: Building an exercise habit. You identified triggers, tracked progress, described routines, and maintained momentum.
  • Evolution: Building habit → Identify triggers → Track progress → Describe routines → Maintain momentum → Achieve goals.
