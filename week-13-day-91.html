The Real Negotiation Loop

The 5-step process for real-world negotiation

1
PREPARE (Research BATNA & ZOPA)
How do I prepare? Research your BATNA (Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement) and ZOPA (Zone of Possible Agreement). Know your walk-away point.
2
OPEN (Open confidently)
How do I open? "I'd like to discuss..." / "I'd like to explore..." Open confidently, not hesitantly. Set the tone.
3
EXPLORE (Explore interests creatively)
How do I explore? "What's most important to you?" / "What would make this work for you?" Explore underlying interests, not just positions.
4
PROPOSE (Propose win-win solutions)
How do I propose? "What if we..." / "Would you be open to..." Propose creative win-win solutions, not win-lose.
5
CLOSE (Close confidently)
How do I close? "Let's agree on..." / "Let's finalize..." Close confidently, confirm agreement, and set next steps.

3 Common Mistakes

Traps that destroy real negotiations

Mistake 1: Negotiating Without Preparation

❌ "I'll just ask for what I want and see what happens."
✅ "I've researched my BATNA and ZOPA. I know my walk-away point and what's reasonable based on market data."
Why: Negotiating without preparation leads to weak outcomes. Native C1 speakers always research BATNA and ZOPA before negotiating.

Mistake 2: Focusing on Positions, Not Interests

❌ "I want $X." / "I need $X."
✅ "What's most important to you in this? What would make this work for you?"
Why: Focusing on positions creates win-lose. Exploring interests creates win-win opportunities.

Mistake 3: Closing Without Confirmation

❌ "OK, let's do it." (without confirming details)
✅ "Great! Let's finalize this. Let me confirm what we've agreed on: [summary]. Let's set next steps."
Why: Closing without confirmation leads to misunderstandings. Native C1 speakers always confirm and set next steps.

🏠 Case 1: Life - Negotiating Rent with Landlord

Context: You're renewing your lease and want to negotiate a lower rent. You've researched comparable units and know your BATNA (another apartment you've seen).

You: "Hi [Landlord], thanks for meeting with me. I'd like to discuss the rent renewal and explore if there's room to move on this. I've done some research on comparable units in the area, and I think there's potential for a win-win here."

Landlord: "Sure, I'm open to discussing it. What are you thinking?"

You: "Based on my research, comparable units in the area are renting for about 10% less than my current rate. I've been a reliable tenant for 2 years, always paying on time. What's most important to you in this — the rent amount or the lease term?"

Landlord: "Well, the rent is important, but having a reliable long-term tenant is also valuable."

You: "That makes sense. What if we signed a longer lease — say 18 months instead of 12 — in exchange for a 10% reduction in the monthly rate? That way you get a longer-term reliable tenant, and I get a rate that's more in line with the market."

Landlord: "Hmm, that's interesting. Let me think about that."

You: "Totally understandable. Take your time. I'm happy to discuss any other ideas you might have as well."

Landlord: "Actually, I think that could work. Let's do 18 months at the reduced rate."

You: "Great! Let's finalize this. Let me confirm: 18-month lease at [reduced rate], starting [date]. I'll send over the signed lease by Friday, and you'll send over the updated lease agreement by Wednesday. Does that sound good?"

Landlord: "Sounds perfect. I'll send the updated agreement by Wednesday."

You: "Perfect! Thanks for being open to this. Looking forward to continuing as your tenant."

Task 1: Negotiating Rent Email

Context: You're renewing your lease and want to negotiate a lower rent. Write an email to your landlord using the 5-step negotiation loop.

  • Use 3 collocations from this lesson

Integrated Dialogues (Long Format - 15-20 turns)

💬 Integrated Dialogues (15-20 turns with evolution)

🏠 Dialogue 1: Negotiating Rent (Life)

Context: You're renewing your lease and want to negotiate a lower rent. Conversation evolves through preparation → opening → exploration → proposal → closing.
You:
"Hi [Landlord], thanks for meeting with me. I'd like to discuss the rent renewal and explore if there's room to move on this. I've done some research on comparable units in the area, and I think there's potential for a win-win here." Open
Landlord:
"Sure, I'm open to discussing it. What are you thinking?"
You:
"Based on my research, comparable units in the area are renting for about 10% less than my current rate. I've been a reliable tenant for 2 years, always paying on time. What's most important to you in this — the rent amount or the lease term?" Explore
Landlord:
"Well, the rent is important, but having a reliable long-term tenant is also valuable."
You:
"That makes sense. What if we signed a longer lease — say 18 months instead of 12 — in exchange for a 10% reduction in the monthly rate? That way you get a longer-term reliable tenant, and I get a rate that's more in line with the market." Propose
Landlord:
"Hmm, that's interesting. Let me think about that."
You:
"Totally understandable. Take your time. I'm happy to discuss any other ideas you might have as well." Explore
Landlord:
"Actually, I think that could work. Let's do 18 months at the reduced rate."
You:
"Great! Let's finalize this. Let me confirm: 18-month lease at [reduced rate], starting [date]. I'll send over the signed lease by Friday, and you'll send over the updated lease agreement by Wednesday. Does that sound good?" Close
Landlord:
"Sounds perfect. I'll send the updated agreement by Wednesday."
You:
"Perfect! Thanks for being open to this. Looking forward to continuing as your tenant." Close

Decision Point Analysis

  • Decision: Combined Open (confident) + Explore (interests) + Propose (win-win) + Close (confirm + next steps).
  • Alternative Decision: Negotiate without preparation or close without confirmation. (Risk: Weak outcome or misunderstandings).
  • Why this decision? Preparation shows leverage. Opening confidently sets tone. Exploring interests creates win-win. Closing with confirmation prevents misunderstandings.
  • Resistance handled: Landlord needed time to think. You gave space and remained open.
  • Evolution: Preparation → Open → Explore → Propose → Close → Confirm.

Strategic Grammar in Context (5 C1 Patterns)

📝 Strategic Grammar Toolkit (5 C1 Patterns)

Pattern 1: Opening with "I'd like to discuss..."

Used to open negotiations confidently. These phrases set a confident tone and show preparation.
  • "I'd like to discuss [topic] and explore if there's room to move on this."
  • "I'd like to discuss my compensation and explore..."
  • "I'd like to discuss the pricing and explore..."
  • "I'd like to discuss the timeline and explore..."
  • "I'd like to discuss a potential partnership and explore..."
Common Mistake: "Can we talk about...?" (Hesitant, not confident)
Native Alternative: "I'd like to discuss [topic] and explore if there's room to move on this."

Pattern 2: Exploring with "What's most important to you?"

Used to explore underlying interests. These phrases uncover hidden interests to create win-win.
  • "What's most important to you in this?"
  • "What's most important to you in structuring my compensation?"
  • "What matters most to you in this?"
  • "What's the priority for you in this?"
  • "What's most important to you in this arrangement?"
Common Mistake: "What do you want?" (Too direct, focuses on positions)
Native Alternative: "What's most important to you in this?"

Pattern 3: Proposing with "What if we..."

Used to propose creative win-win solutions. These phrases create creative win-win solutions.
  • "What if we [creative solution]?"
  • "What if we signed a longer lease in exchange for a lower rate?"
  • "What if we structured the compensation with a higher base and bonus?"
  • "What if we committed to a longer-term contract for better pricing?"
  • "What if we phased the deliverables to meet critical deadlines?"
Common Mistake: "How about...?" (Less creative, less win-win)
Native Alternative: "What if we [creative solution]?"

Pattern 4: Preparing with "I've done some research..."

Used to show preparation. These phrases show you've done research and have leverage.
  • "I've done some research on [topic]."
  • "I've done some research on market rates for my role."
  • "I've done some research on comparable vendors."
  • "I've done some research on comparable units in the area."
  • "I've done some research on the scope."
Common Mistake: "I think..." (Without research, no leverage)
Native Alternative: "I've done some research on [topic]."

Pattern 5: Closing with "Great! Let's finalize this..."

Used to close negotiations confidently. These phrases confirm agreement and set next steps.
  • "Great! Let's finalize this. Let me confirm what we've agreed on."
  • "Great! Let's finalize this. Let me confirm: [summary]."
  • "Great! Let's finalize this. Let's set next steps."
  • "Great! Let's finalize this. Does that sound good?"
  • "Great! Let's finalize this. I'll [action] by [date]."
Common Mistake: "OK, let's do it." (Without confirmation or next steps)
Native Alternative: "Great! Let's finalize this. Let me confirm what we've agreed on."
